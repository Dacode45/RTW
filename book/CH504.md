# Chapter 504: What the Crazed Army Knows

The Crazed Army was not mindless in the way Roland had assumed.

He had understood the broad mechanics: witches who had consumed the Pills of Madness, driven by a compulsion bred into the pill's formula toward the ruins of Taquila, forming a force that moved with singular purpose and without rational capacity. He had understood this the way he understood a river — as a phenomenon with predictable behavior, a known direction, something you planned around rather than engaged with.

Phyllis corrected this understanding the same morning he thought he had finished with it.

"They are not mindless," she said. "They are *narrowed.* The pill does not destroy the mind — it focuses it until everything except the primary compulsion disappears. What remains is genuinely purposive, genuinely adaptive within a constrained domain. They can coordinate. They can modify tactics. They can recognize threats and respond to them." She looked at him steadily. "What they cannot do is want anything except what the pill wants them to want."

"Which is Taquila."

"Which is *the ruins of Taquila,* specifically. The compulsion is not geographic — it is object-oriented. The ruins contain concentrations of God's Stone material, Sigil fragments, the residue of three hundred years of accumulated magical presence. The army is drawn to that residue the way a compass needle is drawn north."

Roland thought about this. "Then if the ruins were moved—"

"They cannot be moved." Phyllis was patient, but there was something in the patience that was specifically reserved for questions whose futility she had already catalogued. "The residue is geological. It has saturated the stone. You could remove the visible ruins and leave nothing but bare earth and the compulsion would remain, drawing the army to the same coordinates."

He accepted this without argument. He had learned to accept corrections from people who had studied a problem for three centuries with the same respect he gave Kyle Sichi's chemistry corrections: quickly and completely, because the alternative was expensive.

"The Church intends to use the Crazed Army's assault on Taquila as cover," he said. "Their own offensive planned for the second month of summer — the army creates chaos and draws attention, while the Church's primary force moves on Neverwinter from a different vector."

"That is one interpretation." Phyllis considered. "The Church's calculation is more complex, I think. They want the Crazed Army to reach Taquila, but not to *succeed* there. The ruins contain materials the Church cannot afford to let the army assimilate. The assimilation would substantially increase the army's capability — and a Crazed Army with substantially increased capability is not a tool anymore. It is a problem."

"So the Church wants the army destroyed at Taquila."

"Or halted. They have enough God's Punishment Army personnel to hold the ruins perimeter, and enough logistical capacity to sustain that hold for several weeks. The Crazed Army exhausts itself against them. The Church watches, reinforces as needed, and emerges with the ruins intact and one threat eliminated." She paused. "What they have not adequately considered is whether we might interfere with that calculation."

Roland set his pen down.

"You have a plan," he said.

"We have had three hundred years to consider our options," Phyllis said. "Yes."

***

The plan was not simple, but it was elegant in the way that plans are elegant when they have been stripped of everything that doesn't belong — the way a well-made knife is elegant, not because it is complicated but because it is precisely as complicated as the task requires.

The ruins of Taquila contained, beneath the surface layers that were visible from ground level, a chamber that the fall had not destroyed. The chamber held the original Sigil activation matrix — the mechanism by which the Union had first created the stone-based consciousness transfer. If the matrix could be recovered and brought to Neverwinter, it would allow the Union's stored witches to create new Sigil stones without requiring the Crazed Army's host chain. It would also, Phyllis believed, provide a method for eventually restoring the stored witches to physical bodies — a process she described with the same careful precision she used for everything, but underneath the precision was something she had not let herself name as hope for a very long time.

"The chamber was sealed before the fall," she said. "Standard protocol — we sealed everything that couldn't be evacuated. The seals are God's Stone based. They would suppress magical ability within the chamber itself, but they would not prevent physical access by someone who knew the mechanism." She looked at him. "We know the mechanism."

"You need to get to Taquila."

"Before the Church's God's Punishment Army establishes its perimeter." She said it flatly. "The window is narrow. The Crazed Army is currently approximately thirty days from the ruins at its current pace. The Church will begin moving its perimeter force within fifteen to twenty days of the army's arrival being confirmed. We would need to be inside the ruins before the perimeter closes."

"And to get to Taquila, you need—"

"Your Witch Union's mobile capabilities," she said. "And your First Army's armed support. The Crazed Army does not target witches or their hosts specifically — the compulsion narrows them too far for that level of discrimination — but the route to Taquila passes through territory where other threats operate, and we cannot afford casualties we cannot replace."

He thought about the spring offensive. End of April. King's City. He thought about resources and timelines and the difference between the things that were urgent and the things that were important and the places where urgency and importance collapsed into each other and the choice became unavoidable.

*This is unavoidable,* he thought.

"I need to see the route," he said. "And I need Sylvie."

***

Sylvie spread the survey map across the floor of Roland's office, which was the only surface large enough for it. She knelt at one edge with the practiced ease of someone accustomed to reading the world at ground scale, and she pointed without being asked.

"The direct route from Neverwinter to the Fertile Plains passes the Impassable Mountain range here," she said, tracing the line. "There's a passage — seasonal, but usable in late spring. Past the passage, the plains are open for approximately three hundred kilometers. Taquila's ruins would be—" She looked at Phyllis.

"Northeastern quadrant," Phyllis said. "The city was built at the junction of two river systems that no longer exist. The ruins are partially buried. You would know them by the stone color — Taquila's architects used a particular seam of pale granite that doesn't appear in the surrounding region."

Sylvie absorbed this. "I can scout ahead. My Eye of Truth reads through surface — I can map the chamber access without entering the ruins directly."

"Can you detect God's Stone concentrations?"

"Not precisely. But I can tell you where I can't see, which amounts to the same thing."

Phyllis looked at Roland. There was something in the look that he was getting better at reading — the look of someone who had made a proposal expecting refusal and was recalibrating.

"I need a week," he said. "There are things I have to move before I can move this."

"A week is acceptable," Phyllis said.

After she had gone, Sylvie rolled the map carefully and tucked it under her arm. She was almost to the door when she stopped.

"Your Highness."

"Yes."

"The witch who described the chamber to me — Phyllis — she didn't say it directly, but the way she described the matrix." Sylvie was careful with words in a way that Roland found useful. "I think she believes that if it works, the stored witches could be fully restored. Not just hosts. Actually back."

He looked at her.

"I think she has believed that for three hundred years," he said, "and has not let herself say it to anyone, because if she said it and it turned out to be wrong, she wasn't sure she could continue."

Sylvie was quiet for a moment.

"I'll be ready when you need me," she said, and left.
