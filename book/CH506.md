# Chapter 506: Ashes Returns

Maggie came back in four days instead of three, which was the difference between a fast crossing and a very fast crossing, and she came back alone — which meant Tilly had agreed.

Ashes arrived the following evening, on a horse that looked like it had covered the last forty kilometers at something approaching its limit, and she came directly to Roland's office without changing clothes or, apparently, pausing.

She looked the same. This was always Roland's first observation about Ashes: she looked exactly the same, in the way that mountains look the same year after year — not unchanged, but the changes absorbed into something that didn't show at a glance. She had the quality of someone for whom readiness was not a state but a baseline.

"Tilly sends her regards," Ashes said. She did not sit.

"She approved the expedition?"

"She had conditions."

Roland looked at her. "Such as?"

"Full written report within one week of the expedition's return. Complete record of what was found in the ruins, the Union's current strength and numbers, the activation matrix and its implications." Ashes's voice was flat and precise — she had always communicated the way someone communicates when they have spent years in situations where imprecision costs people their lives. "She also wants a representative from Sleeping Island to examine the Sigil stones before any hosting arrangement is made permanent."

"That's reasonable."

"I told her you'd say that." A pause — the particular pause of someone who has more to say and is deciding whether the timing is right. "She also wants you to know that she does not agree with your spring offensive timeline. She thinks moving on King's City before summer is premature, and she wants you to consider waiting."

Roland absorbed this.

"What do you think?" he said.

Ashes looked at him. She had the quality — rare in his experience — of someone who, when asked a direct question, answered it directly rather than deflecting into what the asker probably wanted to hear.

"I think the timing is tight," she said. "I think your supply chain can handle it. I think you've thought about it longer than Tilly has and with more information. And I think that if you wait until summer, the Church offensive begins before you've taken King's City, and you're fighting on two fronts simultaneously instead of one front at a time." She said this without urgency, the way someone states arithmetic. "But I'm a fighter, not a strategist. Tilly has a broader view."

"Tilly's broader view doesn't include the weapons I'm bringing to the field," Roland said.

"I know." Another pause. "She knows, too. She just doesn't like not knowing *how much* those weapons change the calculation."

He thought about how to explain *how much,* to someone who had never seen artillery at full tactical deployment, who had never seen the Mark I operate, who had not watched three years of steady escalation in Neverwinter's manufacturing output accumulate into a force that was, on paper, categorically unlike anything the Western Regions had produced in four centuries of organized warfare.

"The weapons change it completely," he said. "That's not confidence. That's physics."

Ashes was quiet for a moment. Then: "Then the spring timing is right." She finally sat — the movement of someone who had made a decision and was ready to engage with the next problem. "Tell me about the expedition."

***

He told her in full. The Union witches, the Taquila ruins, the chamber and the matrix, the Church's anticipated perimeter deployment, the narrow window between the Crazed Army's arrival and the perimeter closing. He laid out the route, the personnel, the operational structure he had drafted. He watched her listen.

Ashes listened to operational briefings with a specific quality: her attention was not evenly distributed. She tracked certain elements — personnel, terrain, vulnerabilities — with the intensity of someone who would need to remember them in conditions where notes were not possible. Other elements, the broader strategic context, she received and filed without apparent effort. He had noticed this about her during the Fallen Dragon Ridge briefing and had confirmed it since.

"The God's Stone seals in the chamber," she said, when he finished. "Sylvie can read them from outside?"

"From outside or from a distance. Her Eye of Truth doesn't require direct line-of-sight — she reads through matter. But God's Stone itself blocks her, so she can tell us where the concentrations are, which gives us the chamber's approximate boundary."

"And inside the chamber?"

"Her ability won't work inside. The seals will suppress it."

"So we go in blind."

"Celine knows the layout from memory," Roland said. "Three-hundred-year-old memory, but — "

"From someone who was there," Ashes said. "That's not nothing." She looked at the route map. "You said twenty soldiers."

"Twenty from the First Army. Iron Axe is selecting them personally."

"Good." She traced the mountain passage with one finger. "This pass — seasonal. What's the weather window?"

"Open by early April in a normal year. This year has been running slightly warm."

"Define slightly."

"Barov's weather observers have it running four to six days ahead of the seasonal average."

She nodded. "Then we have the window." She looked up. "Who knows about this outside this room?"

"Wendy, Iron Axe, Sylvie, Barov — for supply purposes only. Phyllis, Celine, and two other Union witches."

"Keep it that way until the expedition departs." She said it without drama. "The Church has had sources inside Neverwinter before. The fewer people who know the route and timing, the fewer opportunities for it to reach the wrong ears."

He had already considered this. Hearing her voice it confirmed the instinct.

"Agreed."

She stood. The decision was visibly behind her now — the expedition was real, the parameters were set, and everything that came next was execution. "I'll need to talk to Celine."

"She's in the Witch Union building. Agatha can introduce you."

Ashes nodded once. She was almost to the door before she stopped — a habit Roland had come to associate with the things people had almost decided not to say.

"Tilly asked about Anna," she said, without turning. "She wanted me to tell you that she approves. Of the engagement." A pause. "Her word was *approves,* which is not the word she used when I asked what she actually thought." The ghost of something crossed her voice. "She said she wasn't surprised, when it came down to it. She said Anna had always been the one."

Roland said nothing for a moment.

"Tell Tilly—" He stopped. Started again. "Tell her I know."

Ashes left.

He sat for a moment with the route map under his hands, and thought about Anna, and thought about King's City, and thought about the particular architecture of a plan when all its elements were finally in place and all that remained was to move forward and not make mistakes.

Outside, the training yard was quiet. Evening had taken the city in its soft, spring way, unhurried, the light leaving slowly from the western rooftops.

*End of April,* he thought. *Five weeks.*

He put the map away and picked up his pen.
