# Chapter 350: Passing On (Part II)

Mayne stood before Alice's portrait and could not move.

He stared at the old man in the cart beside him — studying O'Brien's face for something, anything that might mean this was not entirely what it appeared to be. A test. A parable. A trick of framing that would resolve itself into something manageable.

He found nothing. Only lifelessness.

The eyes that had once commanded rooms, carried the weight of papal authority, pinned men to stillness with a glance — they were still open, but the thing that had lived behind them was withdrawing. Whatever O'Brien still possessed — the knowledge passed down from previous Popes, the centuries of accumulated doctrine — it was there in the shape of his expression the way an imprint remains in cooling wax. The man himself was already somewhere else.

*He wasn't pretending.* The journey was ending.

Mayne's eyes stung. He let himself kneel again, forehead to the floor, and this time the Pope did not ask him to rise — only waited until the full prostration was complete before saying, "Follow me."

The guards helped O'Brien back onto the cart and wheeled him toward the Pivotal Secret Area's gate. They passed through the Secret Temple and entered the long tunnel. Mayne followed closely in the yellow crystal light, each interval of ten paces marked by another dim glow, the end of the passage invisible from the beginning.

When they finally emerged, the floor had become flat polished slabs. The tunnel had delivered them into a chamber. Ceiling, edges, walls — all fitted stone, deliberate construction, clearly old.

"We're beneath the cathedral of the old Holy City." O'Brien perceived Mayne's confusion and offered the explanation quietly. "The design mirrors it precisely, only built the other way around. I call it the Reflection Church."

"Beneath the cathedral." Mayne looked back toward the tunnel. "I never imagined anything was here."

"It isn't a basement — the two structures don't connect. The only entrance is through the Pivotal Secret Area's passage." O'Brien let a small breath go. "The roof is thick stone slab compacted with clay. No conventional method breaks through it."

"Why design it this way?"

"To preserve a secret without destroying it." He paused. "Before a Pope takes the throne, he must come here. He must witness how the Church was founded, how it grew, what it became. And he must fix its true purpose in his mind."

"To defeat the demons," Mayne said.

O'Brien did not confirm this. He said: "No, child. To win the smile of God."

Mayne blinked. The Pope had already gestured to the guards, who began pushing the cart forward along a ramp, ascending. The passage climbed until Mayne recognized, from the proportions of the arches, that they had entered a space designed to mirror the Prayer Room.

The heavy wooden door swung open with a groan. Despite the obvious time since anyone had visited, there was no smell of dust — someone had been maintaining this place without being told, without being thanked, year after year.

"They may only bring me this far," O'Brien said. "You'll need to push me in yourself."

"Yes, Your Holiness."

Mayne took the handles. The door closed behind them. The torchlight from the passage was extinguished. What remained was the soft yellow radiance of crystals set into both walls — the same spacing as the tunnel, but here above each crystal hung a portrait. Where windows would be in the aboveground Prayer Room, these paintings occupied the wall.

Half-body figures. Men and women both, robes magnificent, faces painted with a precision that seemed to exceed what was naturally possible — living, or very nearly. They tracked the visitor's movement down the aisle. Mayne found O'Brien's portrait and felt his chest tighten: the same man, perhaps a decade younger, wearing a slight fixed smile directed at the doorway.

"You found me." The Pope's voice was unhurried. "It was finished half a year ago. At the time I looked somewhat better than I do now, which is why it's relatively presentable. Properly it should only hang after my death." He was quiet for a moment, studying it. "I was impatient. I wanted to see it while I could still see."

"Your Holiness — these are all..." Mayne's throat had dried.

"The noble pioneers. The Supreme Pontiffs of old." O'Brien's voice carried something close to tenderness. "Come forward. I'll introduce them."

They moved down the hall. Each portrait: a name, a term, a contribution. Mayne listened and looked. Under the dim yellow light the tops of the paintings faded into shadow as he passed, so that he met each face in turn — only the lower half of each expression visible, each one continuing to watch him as he walked away.

Halfway through the hall, he stopped.

Women. Several of them — their clothing different from the robes the men wore, their faces striking in a way that did not require favorable conditions or flattery. The kind of faces that existed on their own terms, apart from how a painter chose to treat them.

Each of them had been a Pope of the Church.

O'Brien continued describing them in the same calm tone he'd used for the men — names, terms, contributions — as though this were simply the same category of information.

Mayne walked on in silence.

At the end of the hall, behind the sanctuary, a single portrait filled an entire wall. The four sides were framed in crystals whose doubled light sharpened every detail of the canvas.

He reached it. A woman. Red hair. A great sword in one hand, held the way fighters hold things — no performance in the grip, only readiness. She stood straight, eyebrows slightly raised, lips together, her gaze at the precise intersection of appraisal and invitation: *I have already measured you, and the result was sufficient*. Under that gaze, Mayne felt something pull at his sternum — an involuntary pressure, the body's response before the mind could decide whether to consent.

He tried to speak.

Turned. O'Brien had already knelt.

"She is Alice," the old man said, and his voice dropped to something near a whisper — not from weakness now, but from the care a name like this required. "The First Pope. A Transcendent. She is also called the Queen of Witches."

The ground shifted under Mayne's feet, very slightly.

His earlier speculation had been correct. The first Popes of the Church were witches.

"Why would a witch found the Church?"

"Kneel and pay your respects, child. I'll show you what you need to know."

***

After Mayne had paid his respects, he helped O'Brien back onto the cart.

"Zero," the Pope said — softly, a name rather than a summons.

A woman in white emerged from a small side door, her footsteps barely audible on the stone floor. "I'm here."

Mayne frowned. He hadn't expected anyone else in a space this secret. *A Pure Witch. Raised by the Pope from her awakening, bound to him for life — but a witch, nonetheless.* And it sat wrong that she had known about the Reflection Church before he did — she who had no claim to succession, who served only the man and not the office.

"Take him to the Illusion Room," the Pope said.

"Yes." Zero crossed to the sanctuary and pressed the carved stone Canon on the altar table. A brief flash of light. The great portrait of Alice began to rise slowly upward, revealing a black iron gate set into the wall behind it. Zero removed the crystal pendant from O'Brien's neck, fitted it into the gate's lock, and turned.

A click from within the iron, and the gate swung outward.

She drew out the crystal pendant — and placed it in Mayne's hand rather than returning it to the Pope.

He looked back at O'Brien. The Pope nodded. "Keep it. It will open the Pivotal Secret Temple's library, and the Research Office's hidden door."

"Come." Zero took Mayne by the hand and led him through the gate and into the room behind the great portrait.

It was circular, spacious — perhaps half the size of the prayer hall — with a single floor-to-ceiling window facing the highlands of Hermes. The walls were curved, bare stone, a bench extending along one arc of the perimeter. No furniture. No other feature except that window, and the stone bench, and the emptiness.

When the iron gate had been closed again, Mayne freed his hand.

"Did His Holiness give you permission to enter this room?"

Zero let the question go. "If I don't come with you, who activates the Stone of Illusion? You don't have Driving Power."

The answer irritated him in a specific way — the irritation of someone who was correct about the principle and irrelevant about the present circumstance. *She behaves as if she serves only the man, not the office. That will need to change.* When the scepter was finally his, when the Church's domain fell to his governance, Zero would learn that the Archbishop warranted the same respect she gave the Pope.

"Then begin," he said, and sat on the stone bench.

"Yes, Your Highness." She lifted a panel on the curved wall and placed her hands on the Magic Stone beneath it. "I should warn you — this is the first time I've activated this."

*The first time?* Mayne frowned. She had lived for over two centuries. Had she not participated in the previous papal handover? Before he could ask, the room went white — and then went away entirely.

The darkness was absolute. Not dim, not shadowed — *absent*. No walls. No floor. No bench. Distance ceased to have meaning; up and down merged without explanation into the same quality of nothing. He felt as though some vast creature had swallowed him and was content to hold him there, in the dark, indefinitely.

He reached down. The bench was still there. He stamped his feet. The floor answered. He let the air go slowly from his lungs.

The room hadn't disappeared. Only the light had.

Then, gradually: a brightening from below. The floor returned — polished slabs with a dark blue sheen, each one carved with elaborate patterns. Light came through a skylight that appeared in the ceiling, and through it he could see actual sky, actual clouds. The room filled itself piece by piece: a marble round table, chairs, a globe, glass cups, curtained walls, an ornamental sword and shield, a Megacerops' head hung above the doorway. As though someone were furnishing a room he was already standing in.

And then the people.

Women materialized around the table — one at a time, lifelike, robed, each one settling into a seat as though she had simply been there and now became visible. At the head of the table, facing the gate, sat the woman from the portrait. Alice. Her hands folded, chin up, expression composed. Every other woman's gaze was on her.

The scene was motionless. A painting given depth and dimension. Then it moved.

"Everyone," said a witch beside Alice. "The Experiment for the God's Punishment Army has been successful." Her voice occupied the room the way a real voice would — there was air pressure to it, weight. "They demonstrate extreme aggression, no fear of death, and tremendous physical power. Even the weakest auxiliary witch, once transformed, becomes a warrior who can match an Original Extraordinary in combat. They also disrupt the release of magical power — which means against Mad Demons and Lords of Avernus, who depend on Driving Stones to fight, they carry a decisive advantage."

"The incarnation ceremony consumes witches' lives," someone said. "And not every candidate survives the transformation."

"Insignificant details." The first witch opened her hands flat on the table. "With continued research, the Quest Society will find remedies for both problems."

"Don't be so easy about it." A witch across the table frowned. "We're already short on numbers. Every witch is irreplaceable."

"What is the difference between dying in the ceremony and dying in a demon's hands?" The first witch raised her head. "At least in the ceremony she contributes something before she goes."

"How can you—"

"Enough." Alice said it quietly. One word, barely elevated above silence, and the room stopped. Every woman closed her mouth and waited.

"Mortals cannot defeat demons," Alice said. The calm of her voice was the calm of something that had accepted a weight so heavy it no longer required emotional acknowledgment. "We have two choices. We can do nothing — enjoy this last interval of peace, and when the next demon invasion comes, go into oblivion with nothing to show for the time we had. Or we can fight. We can place our hope in the God's Punishment Warriors. We can make the necessary sacrifices." She paused. "I will not pretend this choice is easy. But as long as a chance exists to extend mankind's survival — the spirit of witches does not break."

She looked around the table. "I am the Queen of Starfall City. I am willing to allow you to choose. Do you want to give up resistance and die in peace? Or do you want to avenge our fallen sisters, drive the demons out of the Dawn Region, and restore what was taken from us?" Another pause. "The choice you make is not only for yourselves. It is for every witch who will come after. For our future sisters — so that they might still see God's smile."

"Those who oppose — stand now."

The silence stretched. One heartbeat. Two. Three.

Not a single woman moved.

The witch who had objected most sharply was the last to still herself — she did not stand, but she pressed her jaw together and brought her hand down on the table hard enough to make the cups rattle. *Hopefully you're right,* her face said, though she didn't say it aloud.

"Then it's decided." Alice nodded, and if she was unsurprised by the outcome it was because she had understood these women before they understood themselves. "What we must do now is convince the rest of the Union."

"They won't necessarily listen," said one of the older witches. "For the incarnation to proceed without resistance, witches cannot continue to occupy their privileged position. The Union must be formally disbanded. The past must be buried entirely."

"I'll convince them," Alice said. "If they cannot accept it, Starfall City will proceed alone, and a new order will be established."

The scene dissolved. Darkness again, complete, and then the room returned — the curved walls, the stone bench, the window facing the highland.

Mayne became aware that his shirt was soaked through. His head swam with a nausea he couldn't locate precisely.

"You look unwell. Shall I help you out?" Zero appeared beside him.

"Open the door," he said, and got to his feet.

He stumbled through the iron gate and across the prayer hall to where O'Brien waited, and fell to his knees before the cart.

"The Magic Stone maps the scene directly into the mind. Discomfort is expected," the Pope said gently. "The first time I encountered it I reacted the same way. Rest a moment."

"Why didn't it affect me?" Zero asked, pressing her cheek against O'Brien's shoulder.

"Because you are a witch, and your body has long been accustomed to magical power." He patted her head with one slow hand. "In endurance, in resistance — witches are everywhere superior to mortals."

Mayne waited until his breath had settled. "That is the Church's origin."

"Yes. After that, Alice led the witches of Starfall City and two other city-states into the war that followed — eventually prevailing, and laying down the new order. Witches were no longer the chosen people. They were declared the corrupted ones, the demon-lured, the fallen. That war lasted nearly a hundred years. History calls it the Battle of Faiths."

"Did she live to see it end?"

O'Brien shook his head. "Not long after she established the Church, Alice perished alongside another Transcendent. The second Pope carried her will forward, leading the Army until the remaining two factions were subdued. But the battle badly damaged all three city-states — they lost effective control over the wider world. The mortals who had no part in the conflict began to settle in this narrow coastal strip, cultivating land alongside the indigenous people. That became, over time, the Four Kingdoms."

He had become more energetic as he spoke. The frailty that had pressed him into the chair had lightened by degrees — his voice stronger, his eyes clearer.

"Afterward, the Church attempted many times to eliminate what remained: the mortal holdouts from the other factions, the scattered groups who didn't belong to Starfall City. But the world order had already taken hold, and the Church lacked the strength to finish the work. This is all because of Alice's early death." He sighed. "Natalia — whom she considered a friend — refused her plan and then attacked her during a meeting. It's recorded in the unabridged Canon. If Alice had survived, the Battle of Faiths would have ended fifty years sooner. With all witches brought inside the Church, unifying the continent would have been simple. No one anticipated the war would still be unfinished today."

Mayne wiped the sweat from his forehead. "Your Holiness — there is something I don't understand. Why were the early Popes all witches, and then at some point it shifted to ordinary men entirely?"

"Zero, you may go ahead." O'Brien was quiet for a moment after she had departed. Then: "Because of weakness and cowardice."

The words struck at Mayne's chest. "I'm — I'm sorry?"

"You heard correctly." Something had come back into O'Brien's eyes — not quite what had been there in the hall when he was young, but a close relative of it: the flash of clarity that still visited old men in the intervals between fatigue. "Alice decreed that the Pope's position must be held by an Extraordinary. But Extraordinaries were rare, and adequate candidates were not always available. So a few prominent non-Extraordinary witches served as Pope during the gaps — and in one of those gaps, a mortal Archbishop who feared what witches might do to him one day decided not to wait for that day." He held Mayne's gaze. "He usurped the position. And after that, all of us were his successors."

Mayne's mouth was dry. "Then all the later Popes—"

"All of us. Cowardly descendants who profited from the witches' sacrifice." O'Brien exhaled something long and slow. "Whatever else happens, the Church must keep this buried." He paused. "The truth of the Battle of Divine Will is recorded in the Pivotal Secret Temple. The responsibility is yours now. Bear it. Carry it forward." Another pause, longer. "Even the choice to give up — that, too, may someday be the wise one."

As the last words left him, his body seemed to release something — the alertness that had returned during the telling, the brief energy, the clarity. He lay back against the cart, eyes still open, and was quiet.

"Your Holiness." Mayne reached for his shoulder. "Your Holiness O'Brien."

He shook it. Nothing — only O'Brien's mouth, moving very slightly, barely audible.

At the last moment, Mayne understood what he was saying.

*Child. I am sorry.*
