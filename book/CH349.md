# Chapter 349: Passing On (Part I)

Mayne stood at the window of the Tower of Babel's highest floor and looked out over Hermes.

Snow blanketed the city below. Since the last large attack by demonic beasts on the New Holy City, a provisional calm had returned to the icy plains — the snowfall lighter now, the citizens of Hermes moving through the streets in something almost resembling their ordinary lives. It should have felt like relief.

Mayne felt nothing but a hollow ache he couldn't name.

During the attack, a platoon of God's Punishment Warriors had sortied from the city wall's side entrance to engage the hybrid demonic beasts head-on. Nineteen wounded. Two killed. The beasts themselves had lain bellowing in the snow for a long time after the warriors hacked off their limbs — the wall's guards had finished them with spears eventually. Nineteen and two: those numbers were manageable. What was not manageable was the timing. The beasts had arrived before they should have been able to arrive, and that fact refused to leave him.

He turned from the window and sat at the table. He reached for the Canon and opened it, though he'd already read it more times than he could count.

"Flipping through it until the binding breaks won't change what it says." Tayfun entered carrying two steaming cups, set one in front of Mayne, and lowered himself into a chair. "His Holiness still hasn't agreed to meet you?"

"Three requests to the Pivotal Secret Area. No reply." Mayne pressed a hand over his eyes. "I don't understand what he could be doing at a time like this."

"Don't worry." Tayfun wrapped both hands around his cup. "As you've said yourself — the Canon was written by the first Pope, and a man who lived four centuries ago can be forgiven for errors. No one can guarantee every word remained accurate across that span of time."

"This isn't an ordinary error." Mayne shook his head. "If the timeline is miscalculated — if we've lost *half* the time we believed we had — then everything collapses. We've been bleeding resources into unifying the kingdom for precisely this purpose: to concentrate all the continent's strength for the demons' arrival. If the interval is half what we planned for, the God's Punishment Army will still be rebuilding after the war ends. We cannot—"

"Lord Mayne." Tayfun set down his cup. "What exactly are you demanding from this? A guarantee of victory? The certainty that you're the right person to carry this?" His voice had hardened at the edges without quite raising. "Do you think you're the only one who wants to defend Hermes? The only one who's afraid of what happens if the demons reach the icy plains?"

Mayne went quiet.

"In any battle, everyone wants to win," Tayfun continued, slower now, the edge gone. "But we can't even promise victory against the Four Kingdoms — how do you expect to promise victory against what comes from hell itself?" He stroked his beard. "Have you forgotten what the Supreme Pontiff taught? Over-insistence and fear produce the same paralysis. What we do is accept what we cannot change, move forward step by step, and understand that the outcome was never ours to control."

*Fear of the outcome.* The words hit their mark. Mayne sat with them a moment and found they were true. He was afraid. Afraid that when the Pope's ring finally came to his hand, he would be insufficient. That he would watch mankind unmade under his tenure.

"I used to feel exactly as you do," Tayfun said, his voice dropping to something quieter. "Then Heather died. And what I understood afterward was that the outcome is always uncertain. The Months of Demons came early without warning, yet we still took Wolfheart City before the snowfall, exactly as planned. And then Heather was gone, and the nature of the war changed — not by our will, but by the world's." He stood slowly, and laid one hand on Mayne's shoulder. "So whether we have five years or ten. Whether we reach the end we planned for or not. You carry the mission forward. Not for the certainty of winning — but because it's yours to carry."

The door closed behind him.

Mayne stared at the empty cup.

Tayfun's reasoning was sound. He knew it was sound. But knowing that the weight was legitimate did nothing to make the weight lighter, and it was one thing to accept the possibility of failure in the abstract — another thing entirely to prepare yourself to shoulder it alone.

The door opened again. A Presiding Judge stepped in. "Lord Mayne. His Holiness the Supreme Pontiff wishes to see you."

Mayne was on his feet before the sentence finished.

"Take me to the Pivotal Secret Area. Now."

***

The Pivotal Secret Area lay deep beneath the plateau: cold stone, still air, quiet that had depth to it.

Walking into the hall of the Pivotal Secret Temple, Mayne was briefly disoriented. No incarnation ceremony had been arranged — and yet every candle in the hall was lit. The combined light sketched warm orange-yellow shapes across the stone walls, and the chandelier overhead was a gathering of small flames, each one steady. It looked like a celebration, or a vigil.

His Holiness O'Brien sat on the throne, watching Mayne cross the hall.

Mayne suppressed whatever was rising in him, approached the throne, and went prostrate with his forehead to the floor.

"Rise, child." The Pope's voice had thinned. It required attention now to catch what he said.

Mayne straightened and looked at him.

O'Brien's face had been overtaken — not by a single event, but by accumulated time. Age and fatigue had reshaped the features, drawn down the corners, placed shadows under the eyes that sleep wouldn't reach. He had not, it was clear, slept in some time.

"Your Holiness, you must rest—"

"No need." O'Brien smiled, and for a moment the old warmth showed through the wrinkles. "I'm going to die soon."

"Your Holiness—"

"Listen to me, child." A labored gesture that asked for quiet. "I know my own body's condition. There is no point in arguing what is already decided." He drew a slow breath. "The reason I called you today is to pass the Pope's position to you."

***

The words hung in the air between them.

Mayne studied the old man's eyes. He was looking for the thing that would tell him this was symbolic, ceremonial — something performed for form's sake while years still remained.

He found nothing. Only stillness. The penetrating authority O'Brien had carried for decades was gone, and what remained behind it was simply a man who had reached his limit and knew it.

*He isn't testing me. He isn't performing. This is the end of his road.*

Mayne's eyes blurred without his permission.

He went prostrate again. This time the Pope did not ask him to rise — he waited, with the patience of someone who had already made his accommodations with time, until the full prostration was complete. Then: "Follow me."

The attending guards helped the Pope onto a cart and wheeled him toward the gate of the Pivotal Secret Area. Mayne followed. They passed through the Secret Temple and turned into a long narrow tunnel cut through solid rock.

Luminous crystals were set into the smooth stone walls at intervals of ten steps. Looking down the tunnel's length, the light faded into a soft unbroken glow and the far end could not be seen.

After a long walk, the floor changed beneath Mayne's feet — flat stone slabs, fitted precisely, the kind of surface built to last. The walls took on right angles, and a chandelier appeared overhead. The tunnel had opened into a chamber.

"We're beneath the cathedral of the old Holy City." The Pope seemed to sense Mayne's bewilderment. "The design mirrors the cathedral exactly — but inverted. I call it the Reflection Church."

"Beneath the cathedral." Mayne glanced back up the way they'd come. "I never knew there was anything here."

"It isn't a basement — the two structures are not connected. The only entrance is through the Pivotal Secret Area's underground passage." O'Brien's eyes moved around the chamber. "The roof is a thick composite of stone slab and packed clay. Impossible to break through by conventional means."

"What purpose does it serve?"

"To guard a secret without destroying it." He paused. "Before a Pope ascends the throne, he must come here. He must witness — not just be told about — the founding, the development, the expansion of the Church. And he must understand its true purpose."

"To defeat the demons," Mayne said.

O'Brien did not nod. He said, softly: "No, child. To win the smile of God."

Mayne opened his mouth, then closed it. The Pope had already signaled the guards to move forward.

The chamber gave way to a ramp and a stairway, ascending. They moved upward until Mayne recognized the design elements around him — the arrangement of arches, the proportion of the doorways. He was inside a replica of the Prayer Room.

The thick wooden door swung open with a creak that filled the room. Despite what was clearly a long interval since the Pope had visited, the chamber held no smell of dust. Someone kept it clean — quietly, consistently, without being asked.

"They may only bring me this far," O'Brien said. "From here, you'll have to push me in yourself."

"Yes, Your Holiness." Mayne took the cart's handles.

The wooden door closed behind them. The torchlight from outside was cut off. What remained was a soft yellow radiance from crystals set into both walls at regular intervals — the same arrangement as the tunnel, but here above each crystal hung a large portrait, positioned where the windows of the aboveground Prayer Room would have been.

The portraits were half-figures. Men and women both, dressed in magnificent robes, painted with a precision Mayne had never encountered — the faces so lifelike that they seemed to be tracking him as he moved. He came to O'Brien's portrait, and his chest lurched: it was unmistakably the same man, younger by perhaps a decade, wearing a slight smile, eyes fixed at the room's entrance with an expression that felt like recognition.

"Ah — you found me." The Pope's voice was unhurried. "It was completed half a year ago. At the time I still looked somewhat better than I do now, which is why the portrait is comparatively flattering. Properly it should only be hung after my death." A brief pause. "I was too impatient. I wanted to see it while I still could."

"Your Holiness — are these..." Mayne's throat had dried.

"Yes." O'Brien's voice dropped to something that was nearly tenderness. "The noble pioneers. The Supreme Pontiffs of old. Come — I'll introduce them to you."

They moved slowly down the hall. Mayne listened to the Pope's descriptions while studying each face in turn. All of them impossibly lifelike — the yellow light left the upper portions of the portraits in soft shadow as he passed, so that he saw them face by face, each gaze following his progress down the aisle.

Halfway through the hall, he stopped.

Women. Several of them — portraits interspersed among the men, their clothing different, their faces wholly unlike one another, and yet each one remarkable. Not in the ordinary way of official portraits, in which wealth and care and good painters conspired to make even plain faces presentable. These were genuinely extraordinary faces, the kind that did not require favorable conditions to be what they were.

Each of them had been a Pope of the Church.

Mayne walked on in silence.

At the far end of the hall, behind the sanctuary, was a single portrait occupying an entire wall. Four sides framed in shining crystals, which doubled their light against the canvas.

Mayne reached it and felt his heart snap upright in his chest.

A woman. Red hair like streaks of burning flame. A great sword held loosely in one hand — a warrior's grip, neither showy nor strained. She stood erect, eyebrows slightly raised, lips closed firm, her gaze falling on whoever stood before her with a quality that was not aggression and not warmth but something precisely and uncomfortably between: the gaze of someone who had already taken your measure, and found you worth the effort of measuring. Under those eyes Mayne felt a pressure across his sternum, the involuntary pull of wanting to kneel.

He struggled to speak.

"Your Holiness, this—"

He turned. O'Brien had already knelt.

"She is Alice," the old man said. His voice was the quietest it had been all evening — careful, as though the name itself required handling. "The First Pope. A Transcendent. She is also called the Queen of Witches."

The word opened a space in Mayne's chest that was not quite air.

"Why was the founder of the Church a witch?"

"Kneel and pay your respects, child." O'Brien's eyes remained on the portrait. "After that — I'll show you everything you wish to know."
