# Chapter 507: Anna

She found him in the workshop at midnight.

This was not unusual. Roland's relationship with sleep had always been practical — he slept when he ran out of things to do, which happened less often than he would have liked. The workshop was lit by two oil lamps he kept pushed to the far corners so the light spread diffusely, and he was bent over the workbench with a set of calipers and what appeared to be a revised breech mechanism, measuring something he had already measured twice.

Anna stood in the doorway for a moment. He didn't hear her come in — the oil lamps masked sound at the edges — and she watched him work with the particular attention she gave things she found genuinely interesting, which was a category that had always included him.

She came in and sat on the stool near the second lamp.

He looked up. He did not look surprised. He had long ago stopped looking surprised when she appeared in places he hadn't expected her, because he had gradually realized that she appeared where she appeared for reasons, and the reason in the workshop at midnight was usually that she had been reading something that required a conversation.

"Celine told me about Alice today," she said.

He set down the calipers.

"What did she tell you?"

"The coexistence doctrine. That Alice believed the separation between witches and ordinary people was a strategic vulnerability." Anna looked at her hands — the hands that could produce temperatures that melted iron, that had bored the cylinders in the machines that armed his soldiers, that had traced the track of a black filament through more materials than either of them had ever named. "She said that Alice spent eleven years and still couldn't convince everyone. That the doctrine was rejected in her lifetime."

"And you're thinking about what that means for what we're doing here," Roland said.

"I'm thinking about whether it can last," Anna said. "After you take King's City. After the Church's offensive is repulsed. After." She looked up. "In three years, Neverwinter has become something that didn't exist before. Something that people inside it take for granted now, which means they've stopped noticing how unusual it is. But the people outside — " She paused. "Most of the kingdom hasn't experienced it. Most of the kingdom still believes what the Church tells them to believe about witches."

This was a real problem. He had thought about it — in the register of long-term planning, the register he kept for things that were true but had no immediate solution — and he had not resolved it, which was different from ignoring it.

"The Basic Laws," he said.

"The Basic Laws apply in Neverwinter. They'll apply in the Western Region. When you take King's City, you can extend them — but a law is only as real as the people applying it, and you can't be in every town and district simultaneously." She said it without challenge, the way she said most things: as a problem she was thinking through, not a position she was defending. "The Church has had four hundred years to build an infrastructure for belief. They have priests in every village, sermons every week, a theology that explains the entire world in terms people can hold in their heads. You have — what we've built here."

"Which is," Roland said, "better."

"Which is better," she agreed. "But it's not everywhere. And the people who haven't seen it will believe the version they've been told."

He was quiet for a moment. The oil lamps hissed softly. Outside, somewhere in the training yard, a night patrol completed its circuit — the distant rhythm of boot-falls fading.

"Alice's mistake," he said, "wasn't the doctrine. The doctrine was correct. Her mistake was trying to implement it through persuasion in an environment where she didn't control the information." He looked at Anna. "Persuasion works when people have access to evidence. When they can see for themselves that what they've been told doesn't match what's in front of them. The Church kept people away from evidence — kept witches hidden, executed, defined as monsters so that ordinary people never encountered one long enough to notice that the definition didn't fit."

Anna was listening with the quality she reserved for things she was going to keep.

"What we've built here," he said, "is evidence. The question of whether it spreads is a question of whether we can create conditions where enough people encounter it that the old story stops being the only available explanation." He picked up the calipers again — not to measure anything, just to have something in his hands. "That takes time. And it takes controlling the narrative long enough to let the evidence accumulate."

"And winning King's City is part of controlling the narrative."

"The largest part," he said. "The kingdom follows the king, and the king makes law, and the law shapes what people encounter. It won't be fast. But it's a mechanism."

Anna was quiet for a moment. Then: "Alice didn't have a mechanism. She only had the argument."

"Which is why she spent eleven years and still failed."

She thought about this. He could see the thought behind her eyes — not stated, not performed, just present, the way her ability was always present: somewhere in her chest, answering without words.

"Celine said something else," Anna said.

"What?"

"She said Alice knew she was going to fail. Not at the end — at the beginning, almost. She said Alice used to describe it as *planting a seed in the wrong season.* She knew the soil wasn't ready. She planted anyway." Anna looked at him. "Celine said she cried about it, once. Just once, that anyone saw. And the next morning she was back to building the Union, same as before."

Roland thought about a woman he had never met, three hundred years dead, who had understood the problem precisely and had proceeded anyway on the logic that failing correctly was better than not trying. Thought about the particular kind of courage that was not fearlessness but the decision to act despite knowing the odds were wrong and the timing was wrong and the world wasn't ready.

"She got the Union to the fall of Taquila," he said. "Three hundred years of resistance. That's not nothing."

"No," Anna said. "It's not."

She looked at the workbench. At the breech mechanism, and the calipers, and the organized scatter of his midnight work.

"What are you fixing?" she said.

"The extractor spring," he said. "The prototype's extraction cycle is slightly slow under sustained fire. Not enough to cause stoppages, but enough to reduce the rate of fire by about seven percent, and seven percent is—"

"A lot, over a long engagement," she said.

"A lot." He smiled. "You've been listening."

"I've been paying attention," she said. "It's different."

She came and stood beside him at the bench, and looked at the mechanism, and after a moment she held out her hand. A fine thread of black flame emerged from somewhere in her chest — not asked for, not commanded, answering the question of what was needed before he had finished forming it — and traced along the inside surface of the extractor housing, measuring by touch what the calipers measured by distance.

"The spring seat is offset by about a degree," she said.

He looked where she indicated. She was right.

"I'll have to re-machine the housing," he said.

"Or I can do it now," she said.

She did. It took four minutes. The result was precise in a way that a re-machining in the morning would also be precise, but four minutes at midnight was its own kind of luxury, and they both knew it, and neither said so.

When it was done, she rested her hand on the bench beside his. Not touching — just near.

"End of April," she said.

"End of April," he said.

Outside, the training yard was quiet. The city held itself in the particular stillness that cities hold in the late-night hours — not absence but waiting, the specific quality of a place that has become real enough to have a shape even in the dark.

"I'll be here," Anna said.

She meant it the way she meant everything: precisely, completely, without ornament.

He covered her hand with his.

They stayed like that for a while, in the workshop, while the lamps burned low.
