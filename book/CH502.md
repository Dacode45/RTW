# Chapter 502: Taquila's Survivors (II)

Wendy heard it out in full before she said anything.

This was a quality Roland had come to rely on in her — the refusal to respond until she understood, which meant that when she did respond, the response was real. She sat at the long table in the Witch Union's meeting room with her hands flat on the wood and her eyes on something slightly above the middle distance, and she let him finish every sentence before she considered it.

Outside, the spring afternoon was doing what spring afternoons in Neverwinter did: cycling through rain and sun in twenty-minute intervals, neither committing. Through the window, the rooftops of the new district were still half-plastered — construction sounds filtering up from three streets over.

"Twenty-six of them," Wendy said, when he was done.

"Twenty-six that we know are still coherent. Phyllis believes there are more in stones they haven't recovered."

Wendy was quiet for another moment. "And the hosts have to be witches."

"The stones won't activate for ordinary people. The process requires existing magical capability."

"And Agatha has been hosting one for three months." Not a question. She had the tone of someone who was rearranging a room in her head, deciding where the furniture went.

"She volunteered. It's not — from her description, it's not invasive. The stored witch can communicate, share perception during direct contact, but she's not controlling anything. It's more like..." He searched for the word. "A very attentive passenger."

"A passenger who has been waiting three hundred years to arrive somewhere useful," Wendy said.

"That's a generous framing."

"I'm not feeling particularly generous." She said it without heat. "I'm feeling like I've been asked something that I don't yet have enough information to answer." She looked up. "Have you told Phyllis and the others that this is not a command?"

"I haven't given them an answer at all yet. I came to you first."

Something in her posture shifted. Not softening — adjusting. "Good," she said.

***

The Witch Union met two hours later, and Roland was not in the room for it.

He had made this decision deliberately. The question of whether to accept Sigil-hosting was not his to impose. He had presented the information to Wendy, and Wendy would present it to the Union, and they would decide as the Union had decided things since its founding: by discussion, by dissent, by arriving eventually at something they could all live with. His presence would bend the outcome the way his presence always bent things, and this particular outcome needed to be undeformed.

He waited in his office instead, reviewing Barov's spring supply estimates — flour stocks, shot counts, powder allocations, the arithmetic of an army that had to reach King's City and hold what it took while still keeping Neverwinter fed. It was the kind of calculation that could expand to fill any available hour, and he let it.

*End of April,* he thought. *Six weeks. The traditional nobles will be plowing.*

He had built a plan around the assumption that the Kingdom's military capability was concentrated, not distributed — that taking King's City meant taking the kingdom, because the kingdom's loyalty flowed toward power rather than dynasty. He had built a plan around this assumption the way he built everything: by testing each load-bearing element until he was either satisfied or had found the break.

The assumption held.

The question was what happened after.

***

Wendy came to him at dusk.

She carried a list. It was written in her hand, in the careful block letters she used for anything that needed to be permanent, and she set it on his desk without preamble.

"Eleven volunteers," she said. "And three who said they needed more time to decide. The others declined."

Roland looked at the names. He recognized all of them. He did not read quickly — this was not the kind of information that rewarded speed.

"Scroll," he said. "I'm surprised."

"She said she's been making herself available to ancient knowledge her entire life. She saw no reason to stop now." Wendy sat. "Summer said no. She's only been with us for a few months and she doesn't yet trust herself enough to know what she'd be agreeing to. I think that's a reasonable answer."

"It is."

"Nightingale declined."

Roland looked up.

Wendy met his eyes steadily. "She said she wouldn't accept any — her word was *alteration* — to her perception without your explicit consent, because her perception is your primary security system and she won't compromise it without your knowledge. I told her you hadn't ordered anything. She said she knew." Wendy's voice was careful and precise. "She said her answer was no regardless, and she wanted you to know she'd said so herself, not through me."

He absorbed this. The image of Nightingale in his office, saying that, with the particular quality she had when she was telling the exact truth and watching to see if it mattered.

"That's her right," he said.

"Yes." Wendy folded her hands. "I told Phyllis this afternoon. She accepted the list without comment." A pause. "I think she expected fewer."

"So did I," Roland said.

"People trust you," Wendy said. "Not you specifically, in this case. They trust the system you've built. They trust that if something goes wrong, they won't be discarded." She looked at the window. The last light was leaving the rooftops orange. "That's what the Taquila witches need most, I think. Not hosts. Not ability-sharing. They need to know that if they place themselves in someone else's care, that care will be real."

He thought about what Phyllis's face had done when she realized he was still listening.

"I think you're right," he said.

***

The hosting process, Celine explained to Scroll the next morning, was not painful.

Scroll had asked, because Scroll asked everything. She had a reed in one hand and a piece of paper in the other, and she had set both down when she recognized that the answer required her full attention.

"It is disorienting," Celine said. "At first. You become aware of a second perspective — not a voice, exactly. More like a change in how you see the room. As if someone were standing very close behind you." She considered. "Over time, the perspectives become easier to distinguish. You remain yourself. I remain myself. But the boundary is permeable in ways that are — unfamiliar."

"Do you sleep?" Scroll asked.

"I do not require it the way you do. I am present in the stone even when you rest. I observe very little while you sleep — the quality of a stored witch's awareness during the host's sleep is not restful in any conventional sense, but it is quiet."

Scroll absorbed this. Then: "Do you dream?"

Celine was quiet for long enough that it became an answer of its own.

"Not anymore," she said. "I did once. When Taquila was still standing, I dreamed of the city in terms that the city deserved. Now when I have something like a dream, it is of stones. Of what it felt like to be inside a stone in winter when the host's hands were cold." She looked at her own hands — the hands she was borrowing from the young witch who had agreed to carry her until a permanent arrangement was found. "I have not told Phyllis this."

"Why not?"

"Because she already carries enough," Celine said.

Scroll wrote nothing down. Some things recorded themselves.
