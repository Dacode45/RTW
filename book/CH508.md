# Chapter 508: The Expedition Departs

Iron Axe selected the twenty soldiers in two hours.

This was not a fast selection. Twenty soldiers from the First Army's three thousand-odd active personnel — men and women who had trained for two years in conditions specifically designed to identify competence and remove sentiment from the identification process — was a universe of options. Iron Axe's two hours was not haste. It was the efficient application of three years of continuous observation, which was itself a kind of filing system: he knew who he wanted before he opened the personnel rolls, and the rolls confirmed rather than generated the list.

He brought it to Roland's office and set it on the desk without comment.

Roland looked at the names. He recognized most of them — faces from the training yard, from the Longsong campaign, from the Months of Demons defense. The only name he didn't immediately place was at the bottom of the list.

"Yan," he said.

"New sergeant," Iron Axe said. "Promoted last month. I've been watching her for six months. She reads terrain better than anyone in the corps — she maps in her head, continuously, without being aware she's doing it. In unknown country, that's worth two soldiers." A pause. "She's also the calmest person I've found in the First Army. Not courage without fear — she has fear. She's just very quiet about it."

Roland had learned, definitively, not to second-guess Iron Axe's personnel assessments. "Fine."

He added Yan's name to the expedition briefing document.

***

The departure was at dawn, the fourth day of April.

No ceremony. No assembled crowd. Ashes had been clear about this the day before, in the flat and non-negotiable tone she used for things that were not actually negotiable: the expedition's departure was not an event. The fewer people who marked it, the better. Roland had agreed, and the word had been passed through the relevant channels, and the relevant channels had complied.

The departure point was the western gate — the one that opened onto the Kingdom Main Street and beyond it the mountain road. The group assembled there in the grey before-sunrise: twenty soldiers in field kit, Ashes at the front with her sword — the Ashbringer — slung across her back at the angle she had always carried it, as if it weighed nothing. Sylvie beside her, compact and precise, her Eye of Truth a permanent readiness in the slight unfocus of her gaze. Celine in a body she still moved in carefully, the way someone moves in a coat that is almost right but not quite.

And behind them, not quite part of the formation and not quite separate from it, three Taquila witches who had been stored in stones for three hundred years and were now, briefly, mobile. Phyllis had selected them personally: Dawnen, whose stored ability was structural analysis — she could read load-bearing and structural integrity in built environments, which would be useful in ruins three centuries abandoned — and a woman named Reed, who had said very little since her hosting arrangement was confirmed but who moved through the world with the deliberate precision of someone accustomed to limited time in borrowed space.

Roland stood at the gate with Iron Axe and Wendy.

He had given Ashes the operational orders the night before. He had reviewed them with her line by line, confirmed the decision points, confirmed the conditions under which she had full authority to abort. He had done everything he could do from this side of the gate.

*The rest of it is hers.*

Ashes stopped in front of him. The pre-dawn air was cold and clean and smelled of the river.

"We'll be back before the offensive launches," she said.

"I know."

She looked at him with the particular quality she had — the look that was evaluating and accepting simultaneously, the look that said: *I have assessed this situation and determined that proceeding is correct, and I am proceeding.*

"Good luck," he said.

"Don't need it," she said, and turned, and walked through the gate, and the twenty soldiers followed her, and Sylvie followed them, and Celine followed Sylvie, and the three Taquila witches went last.

The gate closed.

Iron Axe said nothing. Wendy said nothing. Roland stood for a moment in the cold and let the silence be what it was.

Then he turned and went back to work.

***

The first week of April held its usual quality: too much to do and too little time to do it in, which Roland had come to understand as the permanent condition of administration rather than a solvable problem.

The ammunition production figures came in on the sixth, and he spent three hours with Barov going through them. The explosive shell count was at ninety-two percent of target. The main-caliber rounds were at one hundred and three percent. The powder allocation was exactly right, which he did not trust and which Barov, when pressed, admitted was accurate because he had adjusted two production schedules the previous week without mentioning it because he had anticipated the question.

"You adjusted production schedules preemptively," Roland said.

"It seemed efficient," Barov said, in the tone of someone who has decided that efficiency justifies most things.

Roland decided to file this particular behavior pattern under *useful rather than concerning* and moved on.

The draft animal situation resolved itself on the eighth — not through procurement, but because a merchant caravan from the eastern territories arrived in Neverwinter with forty-three mules intended for sale, and Barov purchased all forty-three before the caravan master had properly unpacked his manifests. Roland reviewed the purchase price and found it reasonable. He signed the authorization.

On the tenth, a letter arrived from Petrov in Longsong.

Petrov's letters had a particular quality — they were organized the way a precise mind organizes information that is genuinely complex rather than performing complexity. He had been governing Longsong for more than a year, and in that time the city had produced, reliably, the reports and requisitions and administrative documents that kept Roland's information architecture complete. He did not pad his letters with sentiment. He did not bury important information in qualifications. When he had something to say, he said it.

The letter said: *There is a matter I need to discuss in person. It does not concern the spring offensive logistics, which are on track. It concerns information I have received about Honeysuckle family activity that I am not willing to commit to paper. I will be in Neverwinter within eight days.*

Roland read this twice.

He thought about the serial killer, Maans. About the petal emblem visible under a coat. About the person who had hired a former patroller to kill City Hall workers and had enough access to the inner city to meet with him regularly. About Petrov's earlier categorical certainty that the Hull family — the Honeysuckle family — would never move against Roland.

He thought about the difference between a family's loyalty and an individual within that family who had made a different choice.

He wrote back: *Come as soon as you can.*

He put the letter in his security briefing folder — the folder that lived in the locked drawer, the drawer only he and Nightingale had keys to — and he sat for a moment with his hands flat on the desk.

Outside, the training yard was running its morning drill. The rhythm of it came through the window, steady and ordinary, the way it always was. The city around it going about its work, the factories cycling through their shifts, the school in the eastern district moving through its morning lessons, all of it proceeding with the particular momentum of something that had become real enough to continue on its own.

*Eight days,* he thought. *Petrov in eight days. The expedition in twelve to fourteen. The offensive in twenty-six.*

He picked up his pen.

There was always something next.
