# Chapter 505: Two-Front Planning

Barov's report arrived at breakfast and kept Roland at his desk until noon.

It was not a short report. Barov did not write short reports — his natural cognitive register was addition and subdivision, and he organized his findings the way a counting house organizes its ledgers: every column, every subtotal, every discrepancy flagged and traced to its source. Roland had come to rely on this quality the same way he relied on a precision instrument: with respect for what it measured and impatience for what it couldn't.

The core figures were these: the spring offensive required thirty thousand rations for a sixty-day campaign, fifteen thousand rounds of main-caliber ammunition, four thousand of the new explosive shells, and enough powder to sustain artillery operations at sustained engagement levels for twenty days without resupply. The production facilities could meet this by the third week of April. The supply train logistics — river transport to the staging point at Longsong, road transport from Longsong south toward King's City — required an additional forty wagons and two hundred draft animals, both of which were currently below target.

He could get the wagons. The draft animals were harder.

*Always,* he thought. *The last ten percent of everything is always the hardest ten percent.*

He wrote back to Barov with three adjustments to the procurement schedule and a note about substituting mule teams for ox teams on the uphill sections, which the road's grade supported and which would free up enough draft animal capacity to cover the gap. Then he put Barov's report in its drawer and pulled out Phyllis's route map.

The Taquila expedition and the spring offensive were not, on the surface, simultaneous. The offensive launched end of April. Phyllis's window opened mid-April at the earliest — the Crazed Army's arrival at Taquila was the signal that the Church would begin moving its perimeter force. An expedition team needed to be inside the ruins before the perimeter closed, which meant departing two weeks before the army arrived, which meant departing early April.

*Two weeks before the offensive.*

He traced the route with his finger. Neverwinter to the mountain passage, three days. Mountain passage to the plains, two days. Plains to Taquila, four to six days depending on conditions. Call it ten days minimum, twelve comfortable.

He could not command the expedition himself. The offensive required him here, or at Longsong, or somewhere between — not three hundred kilometers into the Fertile Plains with an uncertain return timeline.

He needed someone who could lead a small force through unfamiliar terrain, make decisions without communication back to Neverwinter, protect the Taquila witches while giving them sufficient operational autonomy to complete their task, and return before the spring campaign's final mobilization required all available resources.

He thought about his available options.

He thought for longer than he usually needed to.

***

"You can't go yourself," Iron Axe said. This was not a suggestion.

"I know."

"The timing is too tight, and the offensive will need you physically present for at least the first phase." Iron Axe spread his hands on the table. He had the quality that long-term soldiers develop: he understood logistics not as bureaucracy but as the shape of what was possible. "Who do you have who can lead it?"

"That's what I'm trying to work out."

"What are the requirements?"

Roland listed them. Iron Axe listened with his eyes slightly unfocused — the look of a man sorting candidates against criteria in real time.

"Ashes," Iron Axe said.

"She's at Sleeping Island."

"She's been at Sleeping Island for five months. She'll have opinions about that." He looked at Roland. "She handled Fallen Dragon Ridge without backup and made decisions you hadn't anticipated and they turned out well. She can manage uncertainty. And she can fight — which you need if anything goes wrong in the ruins."

Roland considered this. He had learned, over the past three years, to take Iron Axe's personnel assessments seriously. The man had commanded people in conditions where a wrong assessment cost lives; his judgments had the calibration that came from that consequence.

"Maggie can carry a message to Sleeping Island in three days," Roland said.

"She'll want to come," Iron Axe said.

"She'll need to come. The Taquila witches won't go without someone they trust to make the call if something inside the ruins is worse than expected, and they don't yet trust anyone here enough for that."

Iron Axe nodded once.

"Then you'll need to convince Tilly to release her," Iron Axe said. "Which means you'll need to explain the full situation to Tilly, which—" He paused. "Have you told Tilly about the Union witches?"

Roland had not. He had intended to, and then the past week had moved at the speed that weeks moved when he was managing four things simultaneously.

"I'll write her today," he said.

"And the witch the Union sends with the expedition — Phyllis herself?"

"Not Phyllis." Roland thought about the Witch of the Sands across his desk — that quality of a person who had already given too much to the cause to risk herself on a ground operation when she was the one who understood the Sigil matrix well enough to use it. "Celine, I think. She's been working with Agatha. She knows the activation sequence, and she's been in a host body long enough to function with some reliability."

"And support?"

"Twenty First Army soldiers. And Sylvie — she can read the terrain and the underground structure, and her Eye of Truth will tell us if the God's Stone seals are compromised before anyone enters the chamber."

Iron Axe leaned back. He had the look of a man who had just finished a calculation and found the numbers close enough to proceed.

"It's a thin operation," he said.

"Every operation in the last three years has been thin," Roland said. "We've managed."

"We've managed," Iron Axe agreed. He did not add *so far,* but they both heard it.

***

Roland wrote three letters that afternoon.

The first was to Tilly, explaining the Union witches, the Taquila situation, and the request to release Ashes for the expedition. He wrote it in full, sparing nothing, because Tilly's usefulness to him depended entirely on her trust in his transparency, and her trust in his transparency depended on him being transparent even when it was inconvenient.

The second was to Petrov in Longsong, adjusting the draft animal procurement schedule and authorizing the mule-team substitution.

The third was a set of operational orders for the expedition — preliminary, subject to revision when Ashes arrived — that he wrote in the format he had developed over the past two years: objective, constraints, available resources, decision authority. Clean enough that Ashes could modify it without having to rewrite it from scratch.

He sealed the third letter and held it for a moment.

*If the chamber exists,* he thought. *If the matrix is intact. If Celine can activate it.*

Three conditional clauses between him and a potential transformation of the conflict's entire strategic picture. Between him and the moment when twenty-six witches who had been waiting inside stones for three centuries might finally, fully, be people again.

He filed the letter in the expedition folder.

He picked up Barov's draft-animal shortfall memo.

There was always something next.
