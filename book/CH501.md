# Chapter 501: Taquila's Survivors (I)

The witches of the Union were not what Roland had expected.

He had expected survivors. Agatha had described the fall of Taquila in terms that carved themselves into memory — the golden sword descending, Kraft dying at the gate, the ancient city folding into ruin under the weight of a devil army three centuries deep. He had expected the kind of women that description implied: hollowed, careful, carrying grief the way soldiers carry old wounds, functional but not quite whole.

The three who stood in his office were none of that.

They stood the way people stand when they have been waiting a very long time and have decided to stop showing it. The one in the center — Phyllis, Agatha had said; *the Witch of the Sands, a senior combat ability, do not underestimate* — had the quality of a held note: entirely still, entirely present, giving nothing away. She was slight, with white hair that had clearly not been white for long. The transformation cost. He knew enough about magic now to recognize it in a face. Beside her, the one named Celine stood with her hands folded in front of her, and the third, whose name he had not yet learned, watched the room the way Nightingale watched rooms — cataloguing threat and exit in the same breath.

Agatha stood at Roland's right and had the particular quality she carried whenever she was refusing to be nervous.

"I'll handle the introductions," she said.

"You've already introduced them by letter," Roland said. "Sit down. All of you."

He poured water himself, which he had learned produced a certain effect on people who expected ceremony. The three witches exchanged nothing he could see, but they sat.

"Agatha has told me what she knows," Roland said. "I'd rather hear it from you. How many survived the fall of Taquila?"

Phyllis said, "Of the senior members of the Union? Fewer than I would like to count." Her voice was dry and precise, the cadence of someone who had learned to say difficult things without pause. "When the city fell, we had no way to preserve life in the conventional sense. The God's Punishment Army and the Devils had broken through the outer walls. The Taquila witches who survived did so by transferring their consciousness into the Sigils of God's Will."

Roland had read Agatha's account of this. Hearing it again from someone who had lived it was a different thing.

"The Sigils function as containers," Phyllis continued. "A witch's soul — her memories, her ability, her self — can be bound into a Sigil stone and maintained indefinitely, provided the stone is held by a living host. The host bears the stone; the stored witch persists inside it. They can communicate. In some cases, they can share perception, even ability."

"How many stones?"

"Originally, we did not know. The chaos of the fall was not conducive to counting." The dry quality of her voice did not change. "In the centuries since, we have recovered thirty-one. Of those, twenty-six are active — meaning the witch inside persists and remains coherent. Five are silent. We do not know if the silence means death or merely withdrawal."

Thirty-one stones. Twenty-six witches, each three centuries old, each carrying the full knowledge of the Union's final years, each carrying whatever the fall of Taquila had done to them.

*An entire academy, Roland thought, stored in jewelry.*

"And you've been housed in living hosts since then?"

"There is no alternative. A Sigil left without a host will eventually go dark. We do not know how long it takes — we have not tested it deliberately." She said this the way people say things they have thought about too carefully. "Our hosts have been witches of the Cursed Witch Union — what you would call the Crazed Army. Witches who consumed the Pills of Madness and survived long enough to carry us."

He had known this, from Agatha's letters. The knowing and the hearing were still different.

"You've been living inside them," he said.

"We have been surviving inside them," Phyllis said. "It is not the same thing."

***

Nightingale was in the room. She had been in the room since before the Taquila witches arrived, and she stayed in her Mist, and she watched.

In the fog-world, the three women had the particular brightness of witches mid-power — not actively casting, but the ability present, warm and ready. Phyllis burned the brightest, a deep amber that suggested something banked rather than small. The one named Celine held a cooler light, precise at the edges. The third had a quality she hadn't seen before: her power moved, faintly, rhythmically, as if the ability were breathing.

Nightingale watched Roland. He was doing the thing he did when he was building a system in his head — the stillness that looked like attention but was actually calculation. His hands were flat on the desk. He wasn't taking notes. He didn't need to.

She had learned to read his silences the way she read lies: by what moved behind them.

He believed them. She could see it in the way he held himself — not the careful posture of a man managing uncertainty, but the slightly-forward lean of someone receiving confirmation of a hypothesis he had already formed. He had known, or suspected, or built a structure that required them to be real, and now they were here and the structure was holding.

*Good,* she thought, and said nothing, and watched.

***

"The Crazed Army," Roland said. "Your hosts — they're the ones targeting the Taquila ruins."

"Correct." Phyllis's composure shifted a fraction. Not cracking — adjusting. "The Pills of Madness accelerate magical evolution, but they also accelerate degradation. A witch who consumes a Pill develops power beyond her natural ceiling, but she loses coherence. Over months, the rational mind dissolves. What remains can follow simple commands, sustain basic functions, survive conditions a clear mind could not. But it cannot plan. It cannot reason. It can only *go.*" She folded her hands. "The compulsion to reach Taquila is bred into the Pill's design. When the Church created the formula, they intended it as a weapon against us. A living army that could not be negotiated with, could not retreat, could not be turned — only aimed."

"And you've been riding inside the weapons," Roland said.

"We had no other option."

He was quiet for a moment. Outside, the sound of the training yard carried through the closed window — the rhythm of drills, distant and steady. He had spent years listening to that sound and had come to find it, if not comforting, at least reliable.

"The hosts are dying," he said.

"They have always been dying," Phyllis said. "The question is whether we can find alternatives before the last of them goes silent."

"And the alternatives are?"

She looked at Agatha. Something passed between them that was three hundred years old.

"Your witches," Celine said. It was the first time she had spoken. Her voice was quieter than Phyllis's, and more careful. "The Union's protocol requires a witch host. An ordinary person cannot bear a Sigil — the stone will not activate. But a witch with sufficient magical aptitude can establish the bond, and the stored witch's ability merges with the host's own capability during active contact."

Roland thought about the witches currently in Neverwinter. He thought about what it meant to ask someone to carry three centuries of someone else's consciousness in a stone against their skin. He thought about how to ask that in a way that was not an order.

"I'll need to speak with Wendy," he said.

"Of course," Phyllis said, and he heard in her tone something he had not expected from someone this contained: relief. Not relief that he'd agreed — he hadn't agreed to anything yet. Relief that the conversation had gotten this far without ending.

She had not been certain, he realized, that he would listen at all.

***

After they left, Agatha stayed.

She sat in the chair across from his desk with the posture of someone who had made a decision and was waiting to see if it would hold.

"You've been carrying one of them," Roland said. It wasn't a question.

"Celine," Agatha said. "Since I found her stone in the ruins. Three months." She looked at her hands. "She's — it's not what I expected. She's very quiet, most of the time. She observes. Sometimes she tells me things about the old city that I never knew, even when I lived there."

"Is it uncomfortable?"

Agatha thought about the question with the same care she gave every question that actually mattered. "No," she said. "It's like having a very old, very knowledgeable colleague who is always slightly disappointed that the world has gotten worse."

Roland almost smiled. "That describes at least three people I already work with."

"Celine would be horrified by the comparison." A pause. "She wants to help. They all do. They've had three hundred years to think about what went wrong, and they have ideas. Real ones — not just history, but strategies, plans. They knew the Devils in a way we don't yet. They know what the Blessed Army is capable of, how it moves, what it fears."

He leaned back. The chair registered the shift; the familiar creak of old wood. "What does it fear?"

"Annihilation without purpose," Agatha said. "The same thing it has always feared."

He didn't ask her to explain. He filed it in the place where things waited to become useful, and poured another cup of water, and let the sounds of the training yard carry them both for a moment in something almost like silence.
