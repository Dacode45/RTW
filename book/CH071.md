# Chapter 71: Spy (Part 2)

In addition to the fire burning in the fireplace, Barov had placed a mahogany candelabra on the table. The base split into four branches—one in the middle, highest, with three others arranged in a triangle around it. A burning candle sat on each branch, and together they resembled a bright mountain.

The room hung thick with the scent of pine oil, sweet and slightly rotten, the kind of smell that made drowsiness creep in at the edges. But in Border Town, Barov had long stopped asking for more. In this land of poverty, few could afford anything exquisite or elegant. Here, people counted themselves fortunate to have a roof. Barov had his room, his space—he was lucky enough.

His quarters occupied the castle's former city hall, away from the main courtyard but not far. When the old Lord departed, he'd taken his entire household staff with him. Now the room belonged to Barov alone.

From time to time, he heard voices drifting from the castle corridors and the wind howling beyond the window—two worlds, separated by stone and distance. His old wooden table held books and scrolls stacked haphazardly. On either side, he'd arranged two more tables, forming a U-shape. Usually they stood empty, serving only as display space for his manuscripts. When needed, he would summon one of his disciples and position them at a side table, where they could sort information or draft official documents.

The candles had been changed three times already. Between each replacement, Barov's hand had not paused. Time was precious—far too precious. A stack of documents waited on his desk, and beyond those, the Prince's latest expenditure proposals still required review.

He worked ten hours most days, yet felt no fatigue. Here, in this room, he could marshal all his skills, deploy all his training. His body seemed to possess inexhaustible energy. *This is how it should be*, he thought. No one talking around him. His apprentices working independently, each responsible for their own tasks. No one holding the others back or creating chaos. As long as the Prince's commands were obeyed, the administrative machinery would turn without his constant attention.

*If only the Prince's commands were a little more reasonable.*

Barov gnawed at his lip as this thought took shape. Take, for example, the latest correspondence sealed with Roland's personal seal—the letter sent to Willow Town. In it, the Prince had asked for additional administrative staff and a brig. The reply had been swift: *With the price you offered, you cannot hire the captain, helmsman, and sailors.*

Barov had read it three times, dumbfounded. Without those men, who would deliver the boat? Would they *walk* it back after it arrived? And more pressingly: *why did they need a boat at all?* Trade between Border Town and Willow Town was stable. If the Prince wanted to expand ore shipments after winter, they would only need to send word, and Willow Town would increase their vessels. Purchasing a boat made no sense—the pier was barely maintained as it was, fit only for unloading. Without sailors to maintain it, the boat would rot within months. Was this another of the Prince's inexplicable whims?

As for the request for additional administrative staff, that at least he could understand.

The town hall now operated at full capacity. Barov had brought in more than ten supervisors to oversee the records, manage statistics, calculate income and expenditure. Yet he himself remained responsible for administrative work and legal matters—a contradiction that should not exist. If the Prince wanted to separate these functions, they would need to expand the staff still further.

Under ordinary circumstances, Barov would not have relinquished such responsibility. Every man who held this much power savored the weight of it. *I wanted to become like my teacher*, he had thought once—the kingdom's finance minister, responsible for all of Graycastle's treasury, the King's right hand. *Now only Border Town matters*, he corrected himself silently.

Although Roland had promised him the throne was still within reach, the road stretched long and uncertain. When, exactly, had Barov begun to treat the Fourth Prince as a genuine contender? The shift had happened without his noticing it. Compared to before, it was the difference between the heavens and the earth. *Once, I was certain such an ignorant, dandyish character could never rule a kingdom.*

But since arriving in Border Town, one surprise had followed another. Border Town still stood. Against the odds, against the beasts of the Months of the Demons, against every reasonable expectation, it *stood*. And not through some accident of fortune—through the Prince's strange machines, his impossible plans, his ability to bend people to his will. The way he thought... the way he looked at a problem and saw solutions where others saw only chaos. *He seems more like the Devil who knows everything than a spoiled prince.*

A knock at the door shattered his thoughts. Loud. Authoritative.

"Come in," Barov answered, setting down his pen.

Yarrow entered—one of his better disciples, sharp-eyed and efficient. "Respected teacher, we have caught another mouse."

Barov looked up. "Has he been questioned?"

"He says Timothy sent him." Yarrow stepped forward with a leather-wrapped envelope. "During the search, we found cement powder, some coins, and a letter. We are still extracting the rest of his information. Teacher, what should we—"

"The same as the others," Barov said quietly. "Record his testimony and hang him."

"Yes." Yarrow saluted and departed.

When the door closed, Barov did not return to his work. Instead, he took the letter opener to the envelope's seal and carefully withdrew the parchment.

*The fourth*, he thought.

Before the Months of the Demons had begun, Roland had called him in to discuss something. The Prince believed that once cement powder was revealed, once the new snow powder appeared, once witches were shown to be real—his siblings' spies would become desperate to report. That was when they would move.

The first part had seemed logical to Barov. The second was fantasy.

Border Town held more than two thousand souls. How could one man watch them all? How was such a thing even conceivable?

Roland had answered his unspoken doubt: "We don't need so many watchers. Everyone in Border Town will be our eyes."

Barov had left that conversation certain the Prince had lost whatever wit he'd possessed. Common people did not spy on one another. Merchants did not report their neighbors. Families did not inform on cousins. It was not how the world worked. It *could* not be how it worked.

Winter had come. Roland announced a census. Careful. Specific. Only those who had lived in Border Town for five years or longer. He had stood in the square himself, snow collecting on his shoulders, and explained: Longsong Stronghold still sent agents. They still sought weakness. If anyone saw something strange, anyone out of place, they should report it. The reward was twenty-five silver royals.

Barov had been sitting in this room when the first report arrived. He remembered the cold that had sunk into his chest. Not fear, precisely. Disbelief.

But the report had been accurate.

Then another came. Then another.

By month's end, they had arrested their first genuine spy. Barov had read the interrogation transcript three times, certain he was missing something—some elaborate trap, some trick meant to discredit the Prince.

There had been no trick.

Roland's words afterward had carried genuine triumph: "Let the enemy sink into the bottomless sea of fighting against commoners."

Clumsy phrasing. A boy's words. *And yet.*

The letter in Barov's hands was from someone called "Groundhog." He recognized the handwriting now—one of Timothy's agents, promoted to officer-rank once the Prince had claimed the throne.

The words repeated like a prayer carved into bone: the Fourth Prince had been replaced by the devil. Various phenomena proved it. The signature trembled with fear. Each sentence curled desperately into the next.

Barov set the letter on the table.

He thought of the census. He thought of how ordinary merchants and laborers—people who had spent their entire lives thinking only of themselves—had become the Prince's instruments. How they reported what they saw. How they turned suspicion into fact.

How seven spies had already been caught.

How a single command, delivered in a snow-filled square, had sunk an entire network into the commonplace, into the everyday, where it could not breathe.

Fear or something like it had begun to overtake his skepticism. Not fear *of* Roland. Fear of what Roland was *becoming*.

Barov held the letter above the candle flame. The parchment caught, curled, blackened. The heat warmed his fingertips.

*If the Fourth Prince was truly possessed, if the devil truly lived in him, then he would not fear the God's Stone of Retaliation. The fact that he does not fear it is, by that logic, proof of something. Either he is not possessed.*

*Or the devil has very different plans than the Church believes.*

*Either way*, Barov thought as the ash fell, *I am committed now.*
