# Chapter 72: Holding Court as a King

Timothy Wimbledon sat on the throne, running the scepter through his palm, studying the ministers arrayed before the dais. The pantheon's stone amplified the smallest sound—the shuffle of boots, the rustle of cloth. He had orchestrated this view for years while trapped in Valencia, drowning in the endless disputes of merchants who saw only their own profits.

He stopped. Tapped the scepter against the floor. Once. Twice. The hall quieted.

"You may begin," he said.

Knight Weimar approached first—Sir Ironheart, the militia commander, solid and predictable. "Your Majesty, the raids have proven... robust. Yesterday, several women were taken from their homes. One has since died in custody." Weimar's voice was mild, almost conversational. The edge underneath it was sharp as broken glass. "The outer city panics. We'll lose people to flight if this continues."

Timothy's jaw tightened. He had ordered the hunts. His father's death still made no sense—that strange smile before the rope, despite wearing a blessed Stone of Retaliation of the highest quality, one the Church itself had verified. No man smiled before suicide unless something had broken in him. Unless a witch had *broken* him.

"It was an isolated incident," Langley said, rising. The officer scrambled forward, already cracking his knuckles—a tell of nervousness Timothy had learned to read. "I've punished those responsible. Ten lashes each, twenty-five silver royals in fines."

"One death and three tortured, and you settle that with coin?" Weimar's contempt was absolute. "By what authority did you judge? The former Prime Minister Vic? The Minister of Justice?"

"Extraordinary times demand swift action," Langley knelt, his words tumbling out. "The raids have yielded results—fifteen witches already in custody, under interrogation. Soon we'll know if they conspired to—" He caught himself, but too late. His eyes flicked toward Timothy.

The room had heard enough.

Timothy leaned forward. Every minister knew the truth: his father's death was no suicide, and Timothy would root out the conspiracy that killed him. What they knew and what the outside world *believed* were two different truths. Gerald was the official culprit. The story was written. It would not be rewritten.

"Fifteen witches?" Weimar's laugh was sharp. "The Church found six in the eastern forests. Either your men are extraordinary, or these aren't witches at all."

Langley's mouth opened, but Timothy cut him off.

"Enough." The word fell like stone. Langley was a fool—useful once, now an embarrassment. In better times, Timothy would have found men of actual competence. But the coup had demanded haste, and Langley had been willing. Now the man played at truth-telling in open court.

"You will take these fifteen to the Church," Timothy said evenly. "Pay for a priest to verify them. No torture until confirmation. After that, every arrest requires priestly verification." He leaned back. "Disobey, and I'll have you in the city moat by sunset, feeding the fish."

Langley's face went white. "Yes, Your Majesty."

Once the officer had retreated, Timothy turned to the finance minister. "Compensation for the wronged women—three gold royals each. For the dead woman's family—" He paused, watching the effect land. "—multiple times the standard amount."

Weimar saluted. "Merciful, Your Majesty."

"Next question," Timothy said. The silence that followed was absolute. No one moved. When he spoke again, his voice had sharpened. "Minister Yoshua. My recall orders have been out for a month. What news?"

Sir Bullet—the Diplomat, gray and weary, had served thirty years and would likely die in this post—cleared his throat. "Your sister Garcia has not responded. Your brother Roland replies that when his people are safe at month's end, he will consider his return."

"And?"

"He addresses you as Prince Timothy, not King."

Timothy's contempt was audible in the breath that escaped him. Roland, always the fool. If his brother meant to return, he would learn that kings issued commands, not requests. If he meant to stay hidden, he would learn that distance offered no refuge. Chess had taught Timothy that: every move had a counter. Every refusal had a cost.

"Let him alone," Timothy said, dismissing the thought. "My fifth sister?"

The silence stretched. Bullet's face was pale.

"She's gone?"

"She promised to return. A week later, she and her butler and two maids vanished. My men have found nothing."

Tilly.

The loss struck something raw in him. He had planned for her, expected her—had hoped she would become his adjutant. In childhood, she had outshone him, her mind sharper, quicker. She'd lost the Crown only because she was born female. His father had seen this clearly enough to give her Silver City, a harmless post with harmless prospects. A soft cage.

He should have made the cage stronger. The thought arrived as a physical ache, the kind that precedes rage.

"Restore the former lord to Silver City," Timothy said, his voice tight. "Continue the search. I will not have another person of royal blood wandering among commoners." He turned to his Prime Minister, letting the raging beneath his composure show in his eyes alone. "Only my third sister has refused me?"

Bullet did not hesitate. "Yes, Your Majesty."

"Then we take what she will not give." Timothy's voice was calm again, almost conversational. The hall held its breath. "Duke Ryan guards the South Border. Tell him to move. Garcia will surrender Port of Clearwater and return to King City as my guest."

Marquis Vic smiled—the smile of a man watching a useful puppet perform exactly as predicted. "This should not be delayed. I will have the war order drafted at once."

Timothy nodded to his secretary. The order would be written. The war would be set in motion. Then the doors exploded inward.

A knight in a blue-striped cloak strode down the center of the hall without waiting for introduction. He dropped to one knee before Timothy's throne, gasping. "Your Majesty, news from the south. Your sister Garcia—in five days, she has defeated Duke Ryan's troops and occupied Eagle City." The knight's voice carried the shock of a man delivering the impossible. "She has declared herself Queen of Clearwater. All the southern lords have answered and declared their territories independent."

The silence that followed was absolute.

Timothy's mind did not race. It was simpler than that: one moment his hands held the board, and the next, a piece he had not even known how to value had moved off the edge entirely.
