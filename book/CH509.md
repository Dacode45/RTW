# Chapter 509: Petrov's Report

Petrov arrived on the seventeenth of April, six days earlier than Roland had expected, which meant the information was more urgent than the letter had suggested.

He came without his usual administrative escort — just himself and two personal guards, who stayed with the horses in the courtyard. He looked like a man who had slept in irregular intervals over the past two weeks and was pretending not to. He was good at pretending not to, which was one of the qualities that made him useful.

Roland received him in the small meeting room off the main hall — not the office, which was visible from the courtyard and from the hall's upper gallery. Nightingale was in the room before Petrov entered, in her Mist, present in the particular way she was present when there was information she intended to evaluate firsthand.

Petrov sat. He put his hands flat on the table and looked at them for a moment, which was unusual — he was not a man who usually required a moment.

"There is a member of my household," he said, "who has been in communication with a faction of the Eastern Kingdom."

Roland waited.

"His name is Artur," Petrov said. "He manages the Honeysuckle family's correspondence with merchant factors in the eastern cities. He has held that position for eight years. I trusted him — not because he gave me particular reason to trust him, but because there was no evidence of reason not to, and that combination, over time, becomes trust." He said this without self-pity. It was a precise account of a failure he had already analyzed. "He was the source of the Honeysuckle emblem sighting in Neverwinter."

"He was here?"

"He has been in Neverwinter six times in the past two months," Petrov said. "He was managing the family's commercial interests here — or I believed he was. In fact, he was also serving as a courier for a faction in King's City that has an interest in destabilizing your administration."

"Which faction."

Petrov's hands moved slightly on the table — a controlled adjustment, not a fidget. "You know Timothy is in control of King's City. His hold is not as stable as it appears. There are factions within the noble council that supported Garcia, that supported other claimants, that support keeping the succession in dispute because a disputed succession benefits them directly." He looked up. "One of those factions has concluded that the best way to prevent your spring offensive from succeeding is to create sufficient internal disorder in Neverwinter that you cannot launch on schedule."

Roland thought about Maans. About four City Hall workers strangled with Dreamland Water. About the specific calculation behind targeting people who were about to be assigned to Redwater District — the calculation that public deaths among Roland's civil servants would erode confidence in the new administrative system.

"The Maans killings were their operation," Roland said.

"Artur provided the funding and the contact," Petrov said. "Maans was recruited independently — Artur didn't know his name and didn't need to. The arrangement was intentionally compartmentalized."

"How did you find this out?"

"Artur has a cousin in my household staff who came to me ten days ago with concerns about Artur's spending patterns and travel frequency." Petrov paused. "I investigated carefully. What I found confirmed the concern." He set a sealed document on the table. "This contains a full accounting: his communications, his meetings in Neverwinter, the names and roles of the faction members in King's City that I've been able to identify. It's not complete — I didn't have time to make it complete — but it's enough to act on."

Roland took the document. He did not open it immediately. He looked at Petrov.

"The Honeysuckle family's loyalty is not in question," Petrov said. He said it with the straightforwardness of someone who has thought very carefully about how to establish something as simply and directly true. "Artur acted alone, without my knowledge or the knowledge of any other family member. I want you to know that I came to you directly, six days ahead of schedule, the moment I was certain enough to come."

"I know," Roland said.

In the Mist, Petrov's power burned steady and clear. No deception. No concealment. Just a man delivering, accurately and completely, a report he wished he didn't have to give.

Nightingale stayed quiet.

"What has happened to Artur?" Roland asked.

"He is in custody in Longsong. I did not know what authority to use, so I used my own — he is confined to his quarters under guard, with no communication access." Petrov's voice was careful. "His fate is your decision."

Roland thought about the Security Bureau. About Nightingale's authority. About the fact that whatever happened to Artur needed to happen through a process that was legible — not just to the people in this room, but to the broader administrative structure he was building, which depended on the difference between rule of law and personal authority being visible and consistent.

"I'll send a Security Bureau team to Longsong," he said. "They'll take custody, conduct the formal investigation, and bring it through the legal process."

Petrov nodded. His hands relaxed slightly on the table.

"The names in King's City," Roland said.

"All I have is in the document." He paused. "I should tell you — the faction is not large. It's three or four families at most, acting out of interest rather than ideology. They don't want you to win the spring offensive because a unified Graycastle under your administration is bad for the specific commercial arrangements they currently benefit from." He said this matter-of-factly. "They're not your most dangerous enemies. But they're capable of more of this."

"The disorder strategy."

"Yes." Petrov looked at him. "The theory is that your system is more fragile than it appears — that the new institutions depend on a level of public confidence that can be broken if the right targets are hit in the right sequence. That if City Hall workers start dying and the police can't stop it, people will stop trusting the system and the system will start to fail." He paused. "They're not entirely wrong about the theory. They're wrong about whether your system is as fragile as they think."

"What makes you say that?"

Petrov was quiet for a moment. Something crossed his expression — not doubt, but the quality of someone articulating something he had arrived at through observation rather than argument.

"I've seen what you've built from the outside," he said. "I governed Longsong Stronghold for a year and a half, and for most of that time I was watching your administration develop as a point of comparison. What you have in Neverwinter is not a system that works because of you specifically — it works because enough people inside it have internalized the logic. The factories run their three shifts because the workers understand the schedule benefits them. The police investigate crimes because the investigators believe that's what they're for. The Witch Union functions because the witches — all of them, even the skeptical ones — trust the structure more than they fear its failure." He looked at his hands again, briefly. "That's not fragile. That's the opposite of fragile."

Roland looked at him for a moment.

"You've been thinking about this," Roland said.

"I've been governing," Petrov said. "It's the same thing."

***

Nightingale found Roland in the corridor after Petrov had gone.

She was visible — she came out of her Mist at the corner, which was where she usually appeared when she had things to say that she intended him to hear before he had time to develop his own interpretation.

"He wasn't lying," she said.

"I know."

"Not about any of it." She fell into step beside him. "The part about Artur — there was a moment when he described finding out. The way he held himself." She didn't elaborate. She never elaborated when the thing she was describing was clear enough on its own. "It genuinely cost him something."

"I know that too."

"Do you trust him?"

Roland thought about this for half a corridor.

"I trust that his interests are aligned with mine in most of the ways that matter," he said. "I trust that he is competent and honest and that his threshold for disloyalty is higher than his threshold for self-interest, which for a noble administrator is about as much as you can ask." He paused. "Is that trust?"

"In a practical sense," Nightingale said.

"Then yes."

She was quiet for the rest of the corridor. At the turn toward his office, she stopped.

"The names in King's City," she said. "The faction."

"I'll read the document tonight."

"When you do —" She paused. "Some of them will be in King's City when you take it. If the spring offensive works the way you've planned."

"Yes."

"What are you going to do with them?"

Roland thought about the open trial. About the noble prisoners from the Western Region — the choice he had given them: mine or teaching, not execution. About the Basic Laws and the principle underneath them, which was that people were more useful alive than dead if they could be made to behave differently, and that making people behave differently was a function of incentive structures and visible consequences and time.

"Depends on what they did," he said. "And whether they can be useful."

Nightingale absorbed this.

"The Maans killings," she said. "Four people are dead."

"Yes."

"Artur funded that."

"Yes."

She said nothing more. She didn't need to. The question she was asking without asking was one he had already asked himself, and his answer to it had the same texture as all his answers to that category of question: imperfect, necessary, real.

"The Security Bureau process exists for this," he said. "It'll be handled through the process."

She nodded once. She turned and walked back the way she'd come.

He stood for a moment at the turn of the corridor.

*Twenty-three days to end of April,* he thought.

He went to read Petrov's document.
