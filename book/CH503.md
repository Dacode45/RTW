# Chapter 503: The History of the Union

Roland had begun to think of the Taquila witches as a library with opinions.

Not dismissively — he meant it as a precise description. They had been awake, in one form or another, for three centuries, and in that time they had done nothing but remember. The memories were not passive. They had been examined, cross-referenced, argued over in the quiet communication between stored witch and host, refined by the particular discipline of minds with no other occupation. When Phyllis sat across from him and described the Union's early structure, or the political conditions that had preceded the fall of Taquila, or the nature of the enemy they had all spent their lives resisting, she spoke the way Kyle Sichi spoke about chemistry: with the confidence of someone who has tested the claim repeatedly and found it holds.

He took notes.

"The Union formed approximately forty years before the fall," Phyllis said. "Prior to that, witches who survived their Demon's Bite were organized under individual Saintess figures — what you would call faction heads. Each Saintess had her own approach to managing relations with the ordinary population, her own doctrine, her own cadre of loyal followers. They were not enemies, but they were not unified."

"What changed?"

"A Saintess named Alice." Phyllis said the name with a particular quality — reverence that had been worn smooth by handling. "She proposed a single governing structure, a single code. She argued that the only way witches survived the next Devil offensive was together, and that together required organization the individual factions couldn't provide. She spent eleven years convincing the holdouts. By the end, only one faction refused to join."

"The one that became the Fallen Evils?"

Phyllis nodded once. "They did not call themselves that then. The name came later, when it became clear what they had chosen." She adjusted her hands on the table. "Alice's system was not perfect. No system is. But it created a structure capable of sustaining coordinated resistance across decades — which the fragmented faction model had never managed."

Roland thought about this. Thought about the Basic Laws he had enacted in Neverwinter last winter — nine articles, each one a load-bearing element of the structure he was building. Thought about the principle underneath them, which was the same principle Alice had apparently identified three hundred years before him: that people who wanted to survive needed rules they could trust more than they needed the freedom to ignore them.

*I'm not the first person to figure this out,* he thought. *I'm just doing it slightly faster.*

"The fall," he said. "Tell me how it happened."

Phyllis was quiet for a moment. Not reluctance — preparation.

"We were winning," she said. "That is the part that is most difficult to accept. In the three years before the fall, the Union had pushed the Devil armies back from the Fertile Plains. We had recaptured three settlements they had held for decades. Our combat witches had evolved abilities specifically suited to anti-Devil operations, and we had developed the Sigils to sustain their knowledge even beyond individual death. We believed we had solved the problem of attrition." She paused. "We had not."

"What did you miss?"

"Volume." The word came out flat and precise, like a measurement. "We had accounted for the Devil armies we could see. We had not accounted for the reserves we couldn't. They were patient in a way we had no frame of reference for — patient in the way that something is patient when it doesn't feel time passing. They allowed us to advance. They allowed us to believe the advance was decisive. And then they released what they had been holding back, and they released it all at once, and the city fell in three days."

Roland had read military histories from his original world. He recognized the pattern. It had a name, in some of those histories: *strategic elastic* — the deliberate contraction before the snap. It was effective precisely because it required the enemy to remain rational about a timeline that was much longer than any individual could comfortably hold.

"Three days," he said.

"The outer walls in the first night. The residential quarter by midday of the second. The Great Hall of Elders — where Alice made her final stand — on the morning of the third." Phyllis's voice was a held line. "Most of the stored Sigils were carried out before the third night. Some were not."

Silence in the room. Outside, a cart crossed the cobblestones.

"The Fallen Evils," Roland said. "The ones who didn't join the Union. Were they present at the fall?"

Phyllis looked at him with an expression he could not read entirely. "You have heard of them?"

"Agatha mentioned them. And Zero — from the Church — has indicated the Church is aware of a faction aligned with the Devils that predates the current conflict."

"Zero." The name produced something in Phyllis's face. "She was one of ours, once. Before the Church's formation — or what the Church built itself on. The knowledge the Fallen Evils possessed came partly from witches who chose the Devils' side." She took a breath that was clearly deliberate. "Yes. The Fallen Evils were present at the fall of Taquila. Some of them participated directly in the assault."

He wrote that down and underlined it.

"Then the Church," he said, "is not simply a human religious institution that oppresses witches. It's built partly on knowledge supplied by witches who sided with the enemy."

"Yes," Phyllis said.

He sat with that for a moment. It didn't change his immediate plans — those were already fixed. But it changed the shape of the problem in the long term. The Church was not ignorant of what it was fighting for. It was not even, entirely, wrong about the nature of the threat. It was working with information that had passed through hands that had held it for the worst possible reasons, filtered through centuries of institutional transformation — and the result was an organization that did real harm in the pursuit of something that had originally been, in some terrible and inverted way, a legitimate response.

*That doesn't make Zero my ally,* he thought. *But it makes her less simple.*

"What does the Union need most, right now?" he asked.

Phyllis said, without hesitation: "Time. And a city that will not fall."

He looked at her across the desk, and thought about Neverwinter — about the walls Lotus had shaped, the guns Van'er commanded, the witches whose abilities interlocked like a machine designed for this specific function, the soldiers who had marched out of Border Town's training ground over three years and had not yet broken.

"I'm working on both," he said.

***

After Phyllis left, Roland sat at his desk for several minutes with his pen still in his hand.

There was a knock.

"Come in."

It was Scroll. She had the look she got when she had been thinking about something at an angle to whatever else was happening — slightly sideways, slightly present.

"I have a question," she said.

"Sit down."

She sat. "Alice — the Saintess who founded the Union. Celine mentioned her last night while we were talking. She said — " Scroll paused. "She said Alice proposed a doctrine she called *coexistence.* Not just between witches and the Union structure, but between witches and the rest of the human population. She believed the separation was itself a strategic vulnerability — that if ordinary people and witches remained divided, neither could resist what was coming."

Roland set his pen down.

"Celine said the doctrine was largely rejected in Alice's lifetime," Scroll continued. "The other Saintesses accepted the Union's political structure but rejected the coexistence argument. They thought it impractical. Too dependent on ordinary people choosing not to burn witches." She looked at her hands. "She wanted me to tell you that."

"Why me specifically?"

Scroll met his eyes. "She said: *because he is already building it.*"

Roland was quiet for a long moment.

"Tell her," he said, "that I know."
