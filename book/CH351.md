# Chapter 351: Illusions (Part I)

After paying his respects, Mayne helped the Pope back onto the cart.

"Zero," O'Brien said. Soft, as though the name itself were a call through water.

A Pure Witch in white entered through a small side door, her footsteps nearly inaudible. "I'm here."

Mayne felt something shift in him — a specific kind of unease. He had not expected a witch in a space this secret. O'Brien raised his Pure Witches from their awakenings, bound them to his service for life — but they were still witches, still something other than what stood in this room in succession. And there was an additional irritant: she had known about the Reflection Church before he did. *She, who held no claim to the office. Who served the man, not the line.*

"Take him to the Illusion Room," the Pope said.

"Yes." Zero crossed to the sanctuary and pressed both hands against the stone Canon carved on the altar. A pulse of light. The great portrait of Alice began to rise — slowly, inch by inch — until it had climbed above the hidden gate behind it: black iron, old and thick. Zero removed the crystal pendant from O'Brien's neck, inserted it into the gate's lock, and turned it. A click, deep within the mechanism. The gate swung outward.

She withdrew the crystal — and placed it in Mayne's hand rather than returning it to the Pope.

He looked at O'Brien.

The Pope nodded. "Keep it. It opens the Pivotal Secret Temple's library, and the Research Office's hidden door."

"Come." Zero took Mayne's hand and drew him through the gate.

The room behind the portrait was circular and unexpectedly large — perhaps half the prayer hall in floor space, shaped so that ten people could stand in a row along its curved walls. No furniture. A stone bench extended along one arc of the perimeter. A single floor-to-ceiling window faced the Hermes highland. That was all. Nothing here deserved to be called a secret room except the fact of its existence.

When the iron gate had closed, Mayne freed his hand.

"Did His Holiness give you permission to enter?"

Zero let the question pass. "If I don't come with you, who activates the Stone of Illusion? You have no Driving Power."

*Correct about the circumstance. Wrong about the principle.* The irritation had a precise shape — it was not about this moment, it was about the pattern this moment represented. When he held the scepter, when the domain was his, she would learn that the Archbishop warranted exactly the respect she gave the Pope, not a degree less.

"Then begin," Mayne said, and sat.

"Yes, Your Highness." Zero lifted a dark panel on the curved wall and pressed her palms to the Magic Stone beneath it. She paused. "I should mention — this is the first time I've activated this."

The first time. Mayne frowned. She had lived for over two centuries. Had she not been present for the previous handover? Before the question formed itself fully, the room went white — and then went away.

The darkness was absolute in a way that darkness was not usually absolute. Not dim, not shadowed. Absent. The walls ceased to exist. The floor ceased to be locatable beneath his feet. Distance itself dissolved: up and down converged, near and far collapsed into a single quality of nothing. He felt as though he had been swallowed — not by any creature in particular, but by the void the universe kept behind everything else.

He reached down, found the bench with both hands, and held it. He stamped his feet. The floor answered. He let the air out slowly.

*The room is still here. Only the light is gone.*

Then: a brightening from below. The floor came back — polished dark blue slabs, each carved with intricate patterns. Then a skylight appeared in the ceiling, open to actual sky, actual clouds. The room furnished itself around him: a marble round table, chairs set in a circle, a globe, glass cups. Curtained walls, an ornamental sword and shield in the middle of the room, a Megacerops' head mounted above the doorway. All of it materializing in sequence, as though he were watching a room remember what it was.

And then the women.

They appeared one by one — lifelike, robed, each settling into a chair as though she had simply always been sitting there and was only now visible. At the head of the table sat Alice, her hands folded before her, chin lifted, gaze steady and directed forward. Every other woman watched her.

The scene was frozen for a moment. Then it breathed.

"Everyone." A witch beside Alice spoke first. Her voice filled the room like an actual voice — there was weight to it, air pressure, the texture of a real person speaking in a real space. "The Experiment of the God's Punishment Army has been successful. They demonstrate extreme aggression, no fear of death, and tremendous strength. Even the weakest auxiliary witch, once transformed, becomes a warrior who can match an Original Extraordinary in combat. Furthermore, they disrupt the release of magical power — which gives them a decisive advantage against Mad Demons and Lords of Avernus, both of whom depend on Driving Stones to fight."

"But the incarnation ceremony consumes witches' lives," someone said. "And not everyone survives the transformation."

"Insignificant details." The first witch spread her hands flat on the table. "With continued research, the Quest Society will remedy both problems."

"Don't be so casual about it." A frown, across the table. "We are already short on numbers. Every witch counts."

"What is the difference between dying in the ceremony and dying in a demon's hands?" She raised her head. "At least in the ceremony, something is contributed before the end."

"How can you say something like—"

"Enough." Alice spoke quietly. One word, barely above silence. The entire room stopped.

Everyone closed their mouth.

"Mortals cannot defeat the demons," Alice said. Her voice carried the specific calm of someone who had already arrived at this conclusion some time ago and no longer needed the pain of reaching it. "We have two choices. We can do nothing — hold this interval of peace, enjoy whatever remains to us, and when the next invasion arrives, vanish from history without having tried. Or we can fight. Place our hope in the God's Punishment Warriors. Accept the sacrifices required." She paused. "I will not pretend this is a simple choice. But as long as any chance exists to extend mankind's survival — the spirit of witches does not break."

She looked around the table.

"I am the Queen of Starfall City, and I am willing to let you choose. Give up resistance and die in peace? Or avenge our fallen sisters, drive the demons out of the Dawn Region, and restore what was taken from us?" Another pause. "The choice is not only for yourselves. It is for every witch who will come after us — for our future sisters, so that they might still see God's smile."

"Those who oppose — stand now."

The silence stretched. One breath, two, three.

Not one woman stood.

The witch who had argued most forcefully was the last to settle — she did not rise, but she brought her palm down hard against the table and held her jaw so tight the muscles showed. *Hopefully you're right.* She didn't say it aloud. She didn't need to.

"So it's decided." Alice nodded. "What we must do now is convince the rest of the Union."

"They may not listen," said one of the older witches. "For the incarnation to proceed without open resistance, witches cannot continue in their privileged position. The Union must be formally disbanded. The past must be buried entirely."

"I'll convince them," Alice said. "If they cannot accept it, Starfall City will proceed alone, and a new order will be built."

The scene dissolved. Darkness came back, and then the room returned — curved walls, stone bench, the highland visible through the tall window.

Mayne realized his back was soaked through. His head spun with a nausea he couldn't precisely locate, as though the inside of his skull had been rearranged without his knowledge and not yet rearranged back.

"You look tired. Shall I help you outside?" Zero appeared beside him.

"Open the door." He got to his feet, moving carefully.

He made it through the iron gate and across the hall to O'Brien. He knelt in front of the cart.

"The Stone maps the scene directly into the mind. The discomfort is to be expected — the first time I came here I had the same reaction." O'Brien's voice was gentle. "Rest. You'll recover."

"Why wasn't I affected?" Zero pressed herself against O'Brien's side.

"Because you are a witch, and your body has long been accustomed to magical power." He settled one hand on her head. "In endurance, in resistance — witches are simply far superior to mortals."

Mayne waited until his breath had steadied and the walls had stopped moving. "This is the Church's origin."

"Yes. After that, Alice led Starfall City and two others into the war that followed. They prevailed, and the new order was established. Witches ceased to be the chosen. They became the fallen, the demon-corrupted. That war lasted nearly a hundred years. History calls it the Battle of Faiths."

"Did she live long enough to see the end of it?"

O'Brien shook his head. "Not long after she established the Church, Alice died alongside another Transcendent. The second Pope carried her will forward until the remaining factions were subdued. But the battle badly damaged all three city-states, and they lost effective control of the wider world. The mortals who had taken no part in the struggle began to settle the narrow coastal lands, working alongside the indigenous people. Over generations, that became the Four Kingdoms."

He had become more present as he spoke — voice firmer, eyes brighter. The fatigue had not left him but had retreated to the edges, and what was left was the clarity of someone who had rehearsed this account many times in his mind.

"After the Battle of Faiths, the Church attempted many times to destroy what remained: holdouts, dissidents, those outside Starfall City's original domain. But the world order had already taken hold, and the Church lacked the strength to finish what it had started. All of this because Alice died too soon." He sighed. "Natalia — whom she considered a friend — refused her plan and then attacked her during a meeting without warning. It is recorded in the unabridged Canon. If Alice had survived, the Battle would have ended fifty years sooner. With all witches inside the Church, unifying the continent would have been effortless. No one anticipated the war would still be unfinished today."

Mayne wiped his forehead. "There is something I still don't understand, Your Holiness. The early Popes were witches — and then at some point this entirely changed. When? And why?"

"Zero, you may go." O'Brien was quiet for a moment after her footsteps had faded. Then: "Because of weakness and cowardice."

The words hit Mayne somewhere unprotected. "I — what?"

"You heard correctly." Something had come back into O'Brien's eyes — not the full force of what he must have been at thirty, but a near-relation of it: the quick precision that visited old men in the spaces between exhaustion. "Alice decreed that the Pope's position must be held by an Extraordinary. But Extraordinaries were rare. Adequate candidates were not always available. So during the gaps, a few prominent non-Extraordinary witches served as Pope — and one mortal Archbishop, afraid of what the witches might do to him someday, did not wait for that day to come." He held Mayne's gaze steadily. "He took the position. And all of us who came after are his successors."

Mayne's chest had gone hollow. "Then the later Popes were all—"

"All of us. Cowardly descendants who profited from what the witches sacrificed." A long, slow breath left O'Brien's body, as though it had been waiting years for permission. "Whatever else happens, the Church must keep this buried." He paused. "The truth of the Battle of Divine Will is recorded in the Pivotal Secret Temple. The responsibility is yours now. Carry it. Continue." Another pause, longer — the kind a man takes when he has one last thing to say and wants to say it exactly right. "Even the choice to give up, in the end — that too may be the wise one."

The words released him. His whole body softened against the cart as though some load he'd carried for decades had finally been set down. His eyes remained open. His mouth was moving, barely.

"Your Holiness." Mayne gripped his shoulder. "Your Holiness O'Brien."

O'Brien's gaze was fixed on the ceiling. His lips shaped something almost too small to read.

At the very last moment, Mayne understood it.

*Child. I am sorry.*
